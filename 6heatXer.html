6  Heat Exchangers

Last week we talked about “internal convection”: we changed the temperature of a fluid with heat transfer through the walls of a pipe. Heat exchangers involve pairings of internal flows: the energy lost by one fluid is an energy-gain for another. That is a simple statement of the First Law of Thermodynamics, but we need more than thermodynamics to design them. If we want to get deep into the details, we need to think about conduction, convection, fins, turbulence… almost everything we’ve learned so far!

6.1 Think First

Recall

  1. From thermodynamics: the energy balance \(\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}) = \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})\) tells us how temperatures must be related.
  2. From convection: how did we define \(h\), and what did “fin efficiency” let us do? How did it let us lump complicated fin geometry into a simpler effective area?
  3. What is the log-mean temperature difference, and why does the “average” temperature difference across a heat exchanger need to be a log-mean rather than a simple arithmetic mean?

Look Ahead

  1. You can make a heat exchanger more effective by adding surface area – just make it longer. But as you keep doubling the length, does effectiveness keep doubling? Or does something prevent it from reaching 100%? What limits it physically?
  2. Can the hot fluid in a heat exchanger leave with a temperature below the cold fluid’s outlet temperature? (Can \(T_\text{H,out}\) be smaller than \(T_\text{C,out}\)?)
  3. Suppose you need to cool engine oil from 120°C to 60°C using water at 15°C. If you’re tight on space (limited room in the engine), how would you compensate?

6.2 Thermodynamics isn’t enough

We studied heat exchangers a bit in thermodynamics, and the problems looked something like this: A hot fluid with specific heat \(c_{p,\text{H}}\) flowing with a mass flow rate of \(\dot{m}_\text{H}\) needs to be cooled from 90°C to 40°C. We use a cold fluid with \(c_{p,\text{C}}\) that starts at 5°C and must not exceed 20°C. What mass flow rate \(\dot{m}_\text{C}\) do we need? The answer comes from an energy balance:

\[ \dot{m}_\text{C}c_{p,\text{C}} (T_{\text{C,out}}-T_{\text{C,in}})=\dot{m}_\text{H}c_{p,\text{H}} (T_{\text{H,in}}-T_{\text{H,out}}). \]

But we can’t just say the hot fluid is leaving at 40°C, we must design the heat exchanger to make that happen.

6.3 Heat exchanger design variables

Let’s think intuitively about the ingredients in a heat exchanger design.

  1. The “thermal mass” (or “thermal capacity”) of the fluid is important: \(\dot{m}c_p\). A fluid with a large specific heat will not change temperature as readily as one with a small specific heat. And if we have more flow, it is going to take more energy to change its temperature.
  2. The surface area is important. As is always the case in heat transfer, greater surface area means greater heat transfer.
  3. Thick walls separating the hot and cold flows will increase thermal resistance. We don’t want the fluids separated by 2 cm of plastic, we want them separated by 0.5 mm of copper.
  4. Fins and turbulence will help. More surface area, more mixing, more heat transfer.
  5. Achieving the biggest \(\Delta T\), in general, will lead to more heat transfer.

We are going to move towards a description that looks a lot like the standard convection

\[ \dot{Q}=hA \cdot \Delta T \]

but of course there are complications: How do we describe \(h\) when there are two fluids separated by a solid wall, possibly with fins on it? And what is the right \(\Delta T\) to use? The temperature difference is going to be changing as we move through the heat exchanger… the cold fluid warms up and as the hot fluid cools down.

6.3.1 The Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient \(U\)

At any location in the heat exchanger, we could write a little electrical circuit that describes the heat transfer from the hot fluid to the cold fluid. (I.e., we are considering a particular point in the heat exchanger where the fluid at \(T_\text{H}\) is moving up and the fluid on the other side at \(T_\text{C}\) is moving down, separated by a composite solid surface.)

The resistor diagram represents a cross-section through two adjacent flow channels – one hot, one cold – separated by a solid wall with two layers, with fins on the cold side:

Heat flows left to right: the hot fluid convects to the left wall surface with coefficient \(h_\mathrm{H}\), then conducts through the two wall layers (\(L_1/(k_1 A)\) and \(L_2/(k_2 A)\) in series). On the cold side, the same coefficient \(h_\mathrm{C}\) drives heat transfer along two parallel paths – over the fin surfaces and over the exposed base area between fins. The fins expand the total cold-side area dramatically, which is the whole point of having them.

Rather than carrying around all of the terms in our calculations, we summarize into an “overall heat transfer coefficient” \(U\), defined such that

\[ \frac{1}{UA} = R_\text{total} = \frac{1}{h_\text{H} A} + \frac{L_1}{k_1 A} + ... . \]

Notice that \(U\) will have the same units as \(h\)… we are going to use \(UA\) in our convection equation instead of \(hA\).

Tip

It is common to assume that the areas of all the layers are the same, but that is not always true: e.g. if we have circular pipes with a wall thickness.

It is sometimes reasonable to claim the walls have no thermal resistance: thin sheets of metal are not going to hinder the heat transfer that much. Then we just focus on the convection elements (possibly including fins).

6.3.2 Flow Configurations

TipVideo

Start with this linked video, which describes the co-flow and counter-flow configurations.

We know that the amount of heat transfer depends on the temperature difference.

The “co-flow” or “parallel-flow” configuration

We could have it where the cold inlet and hot inlet are next to each other – in such a case, we get a massive (local) \(\Delta T\) at the inlet side… good news, right? Maybe, but the \(\Delta T\) is going to decrease along the flow, and we will have the limitation that \(T_\text{C,out} < T_\text{H,out}\).

Warning

We also might have concerns about thermal stresses in the co-flow configuration. Here’s the basic idea: solids expand when they heat up. If one side of a wall is dramatically hotter than the other – as it is right at the inlet in co-flow – the hot face wants to expand while the cold face doesn’t. Since they’re bonded to each other, bending stresses develop.

In counter-flow, the temperature difference across the wall is much more uniform along the exchanger length, so there’s no single hot-spot and therefore less thermal stress. Thermal stresses matter most for brittle materials (ceramics, cast iron), in thick-walled exchangers, and in applications with repeated thermal cycling (fatigue from stress fluctuations can cause failure).

The “counter-flow” configuration

We have another option: the cold inlet could be paired with the hot outlet, and the cold outlet could be paired with the hot inlet. In such a case we do not get the enormous \(\Delta T\) at the inlet seen in the co-flow configuration, but we will get better average \(\Delta T\) throughout the heat exchanger. This typically leads to better overall heat transfer. We can also have \(T_\text{H,out} < T_\text{C,out}\).

6.3.3 The Log-Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD)

Reminder: our goal is to make it to something like \(\dot{Q}=hA\Delta T\). What is the relevant \(\Delta T\) to use?

The answer is the log-mean temperature difference, which we described when we studied tube banks. The derivation is below, if you are interested.

CautionExpand for the derivation

We can think about a “local” \(\Delta T \equiv T_\text{H} - T_\text{C}\) at a certain location within the heat exchanger with size \(dA\) such that, from a heat transfer perspective,

\[ dq=U\,dA\,\Delta T. \]

We can also think thermodynamically about the change in \(T_\text{H}\) for the hot side over that \(dA\), calling it \(dT_\text{H}\). With a similar definition for \(dT_\text{C}\):

\[ -\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}\,dT_\text{H} = \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}\,dT_\text{C} = dq. \]

If we write, locally, \(\Delta T \equiv T_\text{H} - T_\text{C}\), we can say \(d(\Delta T) \equiv dT_\text{H} - dT_\text{C}\). Then, substituting a rearrangement of the previous result into this equation

\[ d(\Delta T) = \frac{dq}{-\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} - \frac{dq}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}} \]

and bringing in \(dq=U\,dA\,\Delta T\)

\[ d(\Delta T) = -U\,dA\,\Delta T\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right). \]

This is a separable ODE that we integrate from the “in” to the “out”:

\[ \int_\text{in}^\text{out}\frac{d(\Delta T)}{\Delta T} = \int_\text{in}^\text{out} -U\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right)dA. \]

The integral of \(dA\) from “in” to “out” is just the actual area \(A\)

\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{\Delta T_\text{out}}{\Delta T_\text{in}}\right) = -UA\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right). \tag{6.1}\]

(We will reference this equation later.) Substituting the two versions of \(\dot{m} c_p (T_\text{in}-T_\text{out})\)

\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{\Delta T_\text{out}}{\Delta T_\text{in}}\right) = -UA\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}}{\dot{Q}} + \frac{T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}}{\dot{Q}}\right) \]

and rearranging

\[ \dot{Q} = UA\cdot\frac{(T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}) - (T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right)}. \]

That complicated temperature term is defined as \(\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}\), the log-mean temperature difference (LMTD):

\[ \dot{Q}= UA\cdot\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}. \]

LMTD Formulas

For co-flow: \(\Delta T_\text{out} = T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,out}\), \(\Delta T_\text{in} = T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}\)

\[\boxed{\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}} = \frac{(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) - (T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,out})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}\right)}} \qquad \text{co-flow}\]

For counter-flow: \(\Delta T_\text{out} = T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,in}\), \(\Delta T_\text{in} = T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,out}\)

\[\boxed{\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}} = \frac{(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,out}) - (T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,in})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,in}}\right)}} \qquad \text{counter-flow}\]

6.3.4 Summary of the heat transfer rate

We now have \[ \dot{Q} = UA\cdot\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}} \]

where \(UA\) accounts for both sides of the convection, the resistance through the solid wall, and any fins in one term. The log-mean temperature difference \(\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}\) is a representation of the average temperature difference across the heat exchanger. So, ostensibly: if we know the flow rates (related to \(U\)), heat exchanger size (basically \(A\)), and temperature information (\(\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}\)), we can predict the amount of heat transfer that will occur within the heat exchanger.

But… aren’t we still in the same situation as we were in thermodynamics? We can’t just say what the outlet temperatures are! How would we know what heat exchanger to get to achieve the right outlet temperatures or heat transfer rate? We need to come up with a framework to help us make that decision.

6.4.1 Problem setup

A typical engineering problem looks like this: We are trying to cool down something that is hot, like an engine oil. We know its flow rate, we know what the “too hot” temperature is, and we have a goal temperature in mind. And, presumably, we have a cold fluid (air or water) available at a known reservoir temperature. That is, we know \(\dot{m}_\text{H}\), \(T_\text{H,in}\), and \(T_\text{C,in}\), and our goal is to get the hot fluid down to an acceptable temperature \(T_\text{H,out}\). We need to determine \(\dot{m}_\text{C}\) and the heat exchanger design and size.

The NTU “Number of Thermal Units” framework will allow us to determine the possibilities for \(\dot{m}_\text{C}\) and \(UA\) (which is how we mathematically encode the heat exchanger design).

6.4.2 Heat Capacity Rates and “Effectiveness”

From thermodynamics, the amount of heat transfer is

\[ \dot{Q} = \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}) = \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}) \]

where we define \(C_\text{C}\equiv \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}\) and \(C_\text{H} \equiv \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}\) as the thermal capacities. (This will make the math cleaner below.)

If we were completely unaware of the heat exchanger design (didn’t know if it was co- or counter-flow, didn’t know how much cold flow there was, didn’t know how big it was), the maximum possible temperature change for one of the flows is \(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}\). Then the corresponding maximum possible amount of heat transfer is

\[ \dot{Q}_\text{max} = C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) \]

where \(C_\text{min}\) is the smaller of \(C_\text{H}\) and \(C_\text{C}\).

Warning

It is tempting to want to include \(C_\text{max}\) in this definition instead, but consider what happens thermodynamically if \(C_\text{min} = C_\text{C}\):

\[ C_\text{max}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) = C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out} - T_\text{C,in}) \implies C_\text{max}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) = C_\text{min}(T_\text{C,out} - T_\text{C,in}). \]

Then

\[ T_\text{C,out}- T_\text{C,in} = \frac{C_\text{max}}{C_\text{min}}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) \]

which implies \(T_\text{C,out} > T_\text{H,in}\), impossible. It is a similar logic if \(C_\text{min} = C_\text{H}\):

\[ T_\text{H,in}- T_\text{H,out} = \frac{C_\text{max}}{C_\text{min}}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) \]

which implies \(T_\text{H,out} < T_\text{C,in}\), impossible.

Then it is reasonable to define an “effectiveness” as

\[ \varepsilon \equiv \frac{\text{actual heat transfer}}{\text{hypothetical max heat transfer}} = \frac{C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})} \]

or equivalently \[ \varepsilon \equiv \frac{C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}. \]

With this definition:

\[ \dot{Q} = C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}) = \varepsilon\, C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}) \]

and

\[ \dot{Q} = C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}) = \varepsilon\, C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}). \]

This \(\varepsilon\) will contain the effects of heat exchanger size and design (e.g. fins, channel width, etc.).

6.4.3 NTU and \(C_\text{r}\)

The effectiveness (a dimensionless number) is going to be a function of \(UA\) (wall thickness, fins, size, etc.) and the flows described by \(C_\text{H}\) and \(C_\text{C}\). We can encapsulate these effects into two more dimensionless numbers: the “Number of Thermal Units”

\[ \text{NTU} \equiv \frac{UA}{C_\text{min}} \]

and the reduced heat capacity

\[ C_\text{r} \equiv \frac{C_\text{min}}{C_\text{max}}. \]

So we are thinking about the function

\[ \varepsilon\bigl(\text{NTU},\, C_\text{r}\bigr). \]

6.4.4 Derivation of \(\varepsilon(\text{NTU}, C_\text{r})\) for co-flow

The function \(\varepsilon\bigl(\text{NTU},\, C_\text{r}\bigr)\) depends on the flow configuration… let’s derive its form for a co-flow heat exchanger.

Assume \(C_\text{min} = C_\text{H}\). A step in the \(\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}\) derivation (Equation 6.1) gave:

\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -UA\!\left(\frac{1}{C_\text{H}} + \frac{1}{C_\text{C}}\right). \]

We can re-interpret this to be in terms of \(C_\text{min}\) and \(C_\text{max}\)

\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -\frac{UA}{C_\text{min}}\!\left(1 + \frac{C_\text{min}}{C_\text{max}}\right) \]

and, further, to be in terms of \(\text{NTU}\) and \(C_\text{r}\)

\[ \ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r}). \tag{6.2}\]

Let’s work on the temperature difference fraction a little bit, starting by adding zero to the numerator

\[ \frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} = \frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{H,in} + T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}+T_\text{C,in}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}. \]

This can then be rewritten as

\[ =-\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}+\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} - \frac{T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}. \]

If \(C_\text{H}=C_\text{min}\), the first term is just \(-\varepsilon\) and the last term is \(-C_\text{r} \varepsilon\); if \(C_\text{C}=C_\text{min}\), the first term is just \(-C_\text{r} \varepsilon\) and the last term is \(-\varepsilon\). Either way,

\[ \frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} = -\varepsilon + 1 - C_\text{r}\varepsilon. \]

We bring this result back into Equation 6.2

\[ \ln(-\varepsilon + 1 - C_\text{r}\varepsilon) = -\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r}) \]

and rearrange to

\[ \boxed{\varepsilon = \frac{1 - \exp\bigl[-\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r})\bigr]}{1+C_\text{r}}} \qquad \text{(co-flow)}. \]

This is our function \(\varepsilon(\text{NTU}, C_\text{r})\) for this particular configuration.

6.4.5 Algorithm for HXer Design

  1. Gather information. Likely you will have known inlet temperatures for both the hot and cold sides, and a known flow rate and goal temperature for one of the sides. (“We need to heat this up to…” or “we need to cool this down to…”.)
  2. Calculate \(\varepsilon \equiv \frac{C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}=\frac{C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}\).
  3. Find charts/equations to determine the possibilities for \(\text{NTU}(\varepsilon, C_\text{r})\) (these will be given by me).
  4. Then it is economics: consider the heat exchanger cost (larger NTU means a larger heat exchanger), pumping costs (larger \(\dot{m}_\text{C}\) means more cost to run), space constraints, fluid pressures, etc.

Heat flows left to right: the hot fluid convects to the left wall surface with coefficient $h_\mathrm{H}$, then conducts through the two wall layers ($L_1/(k_1 A)$ and $L_2/(k_2 A)$ in series). On the cold side, the same coefficient $h_\mathrm{C}$ drives heat transfer along two parallel paths -- over the fin surfaces and over the exposed base area between fins. The fins expand the total cold-side area dramatically, which is the whole point of having them.

:::


Rather than carrying around all of the terms in our calculations, we summarize into an "overall heat transfer coefficient" $U$, defined such that 

$$
\frac{1}{UA} = R_\text{total} = \frac{1}{h_\text{H} A} + \frac{L_1}{k_1 A} + ... .
$$

Notice that $U$ will have the same units as $h$... we are going to use $UA$ in our convection equation instead of $hA$.

It is common to assume that the areas of all the layers are the same, but that is not always true: e.g. if we have circular pipes with a wall thickness.

It is sometimes reasonable to claim the walls have no thermal resistance: thin sheets of metal are not going to hinder the heat transfer that much. Then we just focus on the convection elements (possibly including fins).
:::


### Flow Configurations

::: {.callout-tip}
### Video

Start with [this linked video](https://canvas.colorado.edu/courses/136150/pages/hxer-configurations), which describes the co-flow and counter-flow configurations.
:::

We know that the amount of heat transfer depends on the temperature difference. 

**The "co-flow" or "parallel-flow" configuration**

We could have it where the cold inlet and hot inlet are next to each other -- in such a case, we get a massive (local) $\Delta T$ at the inlet side... good news, right? Maybe, but the $\Delta T$ is going to decrease along the flow, and we will have the limitation that $T_\text{C,out} < T_\text{H,out}$. 

::: {.callout-warning}
We also might have concerns about thermal stresses in the co-flow configuration. Here's the basic idea: solids expand when they heat up. If one side of a wall is dramatically hotter than the other -- as it is right at the inlet in co-flow -- the hot face wants to expand while the cold face doesn't. Since they're bonded to each other, bending stresses develop.

In counter-flow, the temperature difference across the wall is much more uniform along the exchanger length, so there's no single hot-spot and therefore less thermal stress. Thermal stresses matter most for brittle materials (ceramics, cast iron), in thick-walled exchangers, and in applications with repeated thermal cycling (fatigue from stress fluctuations can cause failure).
:::

**The "counter-flow" configuration**

We have another option: the cold inlet could be paired with the hot outlet, and the cold outlet could be paired with the hot inlet. In such a case we do not get the enormous $\Delta T$ at the inlet seen in the co-flow configuration, but we will get better average $\Delta T$ throughout the heat exchanger. This typically leads to better overall heat transfer. We can also have $T_\text{H,out} < T_\text{C,out}$.

### The Log-Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD)

Reminder: our goal is to make it to something like $\dot{Q}=hA\Delta T$. What is the relevant $\Delta T$ to use?

The answer is the log-mean temperature difference, which we described [when we studied tube banks](4convection.qmd#the-log-mean-temperature-difference). The derivation is below, if you are interested.


::: {.callout-caution collapse="true"}
### Expand for the derivation
<!-- From thermodynamics, we can write an overall

$$
\dot{Q} = \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}(T_\text{C,out} - T_\text{C,in}) = \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{H,out}).
$$ -->



We can think about a "local" $\Delta T \equiv T_\text{H} - T_\text{C}$ at a certain location within the heat exchanger with size $dA$ such that, from a heat transfer perspective,

$$
dq=U\,dA\,\Delta T.
$$

We can also think thermodynamically about the change in $T_\text{H}$ for the hot side over that $dA$, calling it $dT_\text{H}$. With a similar definition for $dT_\text{C}$:

$$
-\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}\,dT_\text{H} = \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}\,dT_\text{C} = dq.
$$

If we write, locally, $\Delta T \equiv T_\text{H} - T_\text{C}$, we can say $d(\Delta T) \equiv dT_\text{H} - dT_\text{C}$. Then, substituting a rearrangement of the previous result into this equation

$$
d(\Delta T) = \frac{dq}{-\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} - \frac{dq}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}
$$

and bringing in $dq=U\,dA\,\Delta T$

$$
d(\Delta T) = -U\,dA\,\Delta T\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right).
$$

This is a separable ODE that we integrate from the "in" to the "out":

$$
\int_\text{in}^\text{out}\frac{d(\Delta T)}{\Delta T} = \int_\text{in}^\text{out} -U\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right)dA.
$$

The integral of $dA$ from "in" to "out" is just the actual area $A$

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{\Delta T_\text{out}}{\Delta T_\text{in}}\right) = -UA\left(\frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}} + \frac{1}{\dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}}\right).
$$ {#eq-ref-later}

(We will reference this equation later.) Substituting the two versions of $\dot{m} c_p (T_\text{in}-T_\text{out})$

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{\Delta T_\text{out}}{\Delta T_\text{in}}\right) = -UA\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}}{\dot{Q}} + \frac{T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}}{\dot{Q}}\right)
$$

and rearranging

$$
\dot{Q} = UA\cdot\frac{(T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}) - (T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right)}.
$$

That complicated temperature term is defined as $\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}$, the **log-mean temperature difference (LMTD)**:

$$
\dot{Q}= UA\cdot\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}.
$$

:::

**LMTD Formulas**

**For co-flow:** $\Delta T_\text{out} = T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,out}$, $\Delta T_\text{in} = T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}$

$$\boxed{\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}} = \frac{(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) - (T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,out})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}\right)}} \qquad \text{co-flow}$$

**For counter-flow:** $\Delta T_\text{out} = T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,in}$, $\Delta T_\text{in} = T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,out}$

$$\boxed{\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}} = \frac{(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,out}) - (T_\text{H,out} - T_\text{C,in})}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,in}}\right)}} \qquad \text{counter-flow}$$



### Summary of the heat transfer rate

We now have
$$
\dot{Q} = UA\cdot\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}
$$

where $UA$ accounts for both sides of the convection, the resistance through the solid wall, and any fins in one term. The log-mean temperature difference $\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}$ is a representation of the average temperature difference across the heat exchanger. So, ostensibly: if we know the flow rates (related to $U$), heat exchanger size (basically $A$), and temperature information ($\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}$), we can predict the amount of heat transfer that will occur within the heat exchanger.

But... aren't we still in the same situation as we were in thermodynamics? We can't just say what the outlet temperatures are! How would we know what heat exchanger to get to achieve the right outlet temperatures or heat transfer rate? We need to come up with a framework to help us make that decision.

### Problem setup

A typical engineering problem looks like this: We are trying to cool down something that is hot, like an engine oil. We know its flow rate, we know what the "too hot" temperature is, and we have a goal temperature in mind. And, presumably, we have a cold fluid (air or water) available at a known reservoir temperature. That is, we know $\dot{m}_\text{H}$, $T_\text{H,in}$, and $T_\text{C,in}$, and our goal is to get the hot fluid down to an acceptable temperature $T_\text{H,out}$. We need to determine $\dot{m}_\text{C}$ and the heat exchanger design and size.


The NTU "Number of Thermal Units" framework will allow us to determine the possibilities for $\dot{m}_\text{C}$ and $UA$ (which is how we mathematically encode the heat exchanger design).


### Heat Capacity Rates and "Effectiveness"

From thermodynamics, the amount of heat transfer is

$$
\dot{Q} = \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}) = \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})
$$

where we define $C_\text{C}\equiv \dot{m}_\text{C} c_{p,\text{C}}$ and $C_\text{H} \equiv \dot{m}_\text{H} c_{p,\text{H}}$ as the thermal capacities. (This will make the math cleaner below.)

If we were completely unaware of the heat exchanger design (didn't know if it was co- or counter-flow, didn't know how much cold flow there was, didn't know how big it was), the maximum possible temperature change for one of the flows is $T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}$. Then the corresponding maximum possible amount of heat transfer is 

$$
\dot{Q}_\text{max} = C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in})
$$

where $C_\text{min}$ is the smaller of $C_\text{H}$ and $C_\text{C}$.

::: {.callout-warning}
It is tempting to want to include $C_\text{max}$ in this definition instead, but consider what happens thermodynamically if $C_\text{min} = C_\text{C}$:

$$
C_\text{max}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) = C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out} - T_\text{C,in}) \implies C_\text{max}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in}) = C_\text{min}(T_\text{C,out} - T_\text{C,in}).
$$

Then

$$
T_\text{C,out}- T_\text{C,in} = \frac{C_\text{max}}{C_\text{min}}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in})
$$

which implies $T_\text{C,out} > T_\text{H,in}$, impossible. It is a similar logic if $C_\text{min} = C_\text{H}$:

$$
T_\text{H,in}- T_\text{H,out} = \frac{C_\text{max}}{C_\text{min}}(T_\text{H,in} - T_\text{C,in})
$$

which implies $T_\text{H,out} < T_\text{C,in}$, impossible.
:::

Then it is reasonable to define an "effectiveness" as

$$
\varepsilon \equiv \frac{\text{actual heat transfer}}{\text{hypothetical max heat transfer}} = \frac{C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}
$$

or equivalently
$$
\varepsilon \equiv  
\frac{C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}.
$$

With this definition:

$$
\dot{Q} = C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}) = \varepsilon\, C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})
$$

and

$$
\dot{Q} = C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}) = \varepsilon\, C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}).
$$

This $\varepsilon$ will contain the effects of heat exchanger size and design (e.g. fins, channel width, etc.).



### NTU and $C_\text{r}$

The effectiveness (a dimensionless number) is going to be a function of $UA$ (wall thickness, fins, size, etc.) and the flows described by $C_\text{H}$ and $C_\text{C}$. We can encapsulate these effects into two more dimensionless numbers: the "Number of Thermal Units"

$$
\text{NTU} \equiv \frac{UA}{C_\text{min}}
$$

and the reduced heat capacity

$$
C_\text{r} \equiv \frac{C_\text{min}}{C_\text{max}}.
$$

So we are thinking about the function 

$$
\varepsilon\bigl(\text{NTU},\, C_\text{r}\bigr).
$$


### Derivation of $\varepsilon(\text{NTU}, C_\text{r})$ for co-flow

The function $\varepsilon\bigl(\text{NTU},\, C_\text{r}\bigr)$ depends on the flow configuration... let's derive its form for a co-flow heat exchanger.

Assume $C_\text{min} = C_\text{H}$. A step in the $\Delta T_{\ell\text{m}}$ derivation (@eq-ref-later) gave:

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -UA\!\left(\frac{1}{C_\text{H}} + \frac{1}{C_\text{C}}\right).
$$

We can re-interpret this to be in terms of $C_\text{min}$ and $C_\text{max}$

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -\frac{UA}{C_\text{min}}\!\left(1 + \frac{C_\text{min}}{C_\text{max}}\right) 
$$

and, further, to be in terms of $\text{NTU}$ and $C_\text{r}$

$$
\ln\!\left(\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}\right) = -\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r}).
$$ {#eq-before-aside}

Let's work on the temperature difference fraction a little bit, starting by adding zero to the numerator

$$
\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} = \frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{H,in} + T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}+T_\text{C,in}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}.
$$

This can then be rewritten as

$$
=-\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}+\frac{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} - \frac{T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}}.
$$

If $C_\text{H}=C_\text{min}$, the first term is just $-\varepsilon$ and the last term is $-C_\text{r} \varepsilon$; if $C_\text{C}=C_\text{min}$, the first term is just $-C_\text{r} \varepsilon$ and the last term is $-\varepsilon$. Either way,

$$
\frac{T_\text{H,out}-T_\text{C,out}}{T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in}} = -\varepsilon + 1 - C_\text{r}\varepsilon.
$$

We bring this result back into @eq-before-aside

$$
\ln(-\varepsilon + 1 - C_\text{r}\varepsilon) = -\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r})
$$

and rearrange to 

$$
\boxed{\varepsilon = \frac{1 - \exp\bigl[-\text{NTU}(1+C_\text{r})\bigr]}{1+C_\text{r}}} \qquad \text{(co-flow)}.
$$

This is our function $\varepsilon(\text{NTU}, C_\text{r})$ for this particular configuration.

### Algorithm for HXer Design

1. Gather information. Likely you will have known inlet temperatures for both the hot and cold sides, and a known flow rate and goal temperature for one of the sides. ("We need to heat this up to..." or "we need to cool this down to...".)
2. Calculate $\varepsilon \equiv \frac{C_\text{H}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{H,out})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}=\frac{C_\text{C}(T_\text{C,out}-T_\text{C,in})}{C_\text{min}(T_\text{H,in}-T_\text{C,in})}$.
3. Find charts/equations to determine the possibilities for $\text{NTU}(\varepsilon, C_\text{r})$ (these will be given by me).
4. Then it is economics: consider the heat exchanger cost (larger NTU means a larger heat exchanger), pumping costs (larger $\dot{m}_\text{C}$ means more cost to run), space constraints, fluid pressures, etc.
