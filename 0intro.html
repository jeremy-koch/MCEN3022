The Three Modes of Heat Transfer

TipVideo

Start with this video link. Then read this section. By consuming the content in one medium, then reviewing in another, I hope it will solidify your learning!

From thermodynamics, recall that the energy of a system is changed via heat transfer (to or from an object) or work (on or by an object). This class will obviously be focusing on heat transfer. Recognize that the Laws of Thermodynamics say nothing about how to determine the rate of heat transfer! That will be what we will be focusing on: determining the heat transfer rate.

We give the symbol \(\dot{Q}\) to the heat transfer rate, where \(Q\) is the amount of heat (energy) transferred and the dot on top references a time derivative. The units of \(\dot{Q}\) are Watts = Joules/second. We’ve got three “modes” of heat transfer to think about in this course: conduction, convection, and radiation. Here’s a little preview…

Conduction

Conduction is primarily associated with solids. At the microscopic level, energy propagates through lattice vibrations – add energy to one atom and it begins jostling its neighbors, passing the disturbance along like a chain of springs. In metals, there is a second and often dominant mechanism: free electrons move rapidly through the lattice and carry thermal energy with them. This is why metals tend to be far better thermal conductors than non-metals (copper conducts heat roughly 600 times better than glass), and why good electrical conductors are almost always good thermal conductors too.

Both mechanisms depend on material structure: alloying, crystal defects, and temperature all affect how freely energy is transferred. We invented a material property known as the thermal conductivity \(k\) that helps us catalog this behavior. The units of \(k\) are W/m·K.

The basic equation of conductive heat transfer is something like

\[ \dot{Q}_\text{conduction} =A_c k \frac{T_1-T_2}{w} \]

though that is a bit of a simplification. As will be the case with all heat transfer modes, heat transfer will be increased by area – here we have \(A_c\) as the cross-sectional area. It will also be the case in all modes that heat transfer increases with increasing temperature difference – here maybe between two sides of an object with temperatures \(T_1\) and \(T_2\), separated by a thickness \(w\).

Convection

Convection is associated with fluids – gases or liquids. If a cool evening breeze passes over you on a hot day, that breeze is removing energy from you: convective heat transfer. Intuitively, we expect that the speed of the breeze will effect the heat transfer rate, and that is indeed the case: we will spend a few weeks learning how to calculate \(h\), the convective heat transfer coefficient, as a function of fluid speed. The units of \(h\) are W/m²·K.

The basic equation of convective heat transfer is

\[ \dot{Q}_\text{convection} = h \cdot A_\text{surface} \cdot (T_\text{surface} - T_\infty) \]

Again we see: an area dependence, here called a surface area \(A_\text{surface}\), and a dependence on temperature difference.

Radiation

Radiative heat transfer occurs any time objects of different temperatures “see” each other – you are probably a bit warmer than your wall right now, and so you are radiating at your wall as you read this. In practice, radiation is often negligible until there is a big temperature difference… the Sun, for example, is about 5800 K, and it is obviously sending a lot of energy our way. The mechanism: it produces photons, which carry energy through the vacuum of space, through Earth’s atmosphere, and to your skin, where the photons are absorbed, and their energy warms you.

The basic equation of radiation is something like

\[ \dot{Q}_\text{radiation} = \varepsilon \sigma F A_\text{surface} \cdot (T_\text{surface}^4 - T_0^4) \]

Again, we see an area dependence, and something that looks like a dependence on temperature difference, but the temperatures are raised to the fourth power. The Sun’s surface is about 5800 K, while your skin is about 300 K… that temperature term is massive!

Thankfully, there is a term in this equation that is basically “how well does the Sun see you”: \(F\), the view factor. It is a dimensionless number between 0 and 1, and would be quite small because the Sun doesn’t “see you” that well. There is another dimensionless number, the emissivity, \(\varepsilon\), that is a material property that describes how good of an “emitter” you are. And this equation includes a constant, \(\sigma=5.67 \times 10^{-8}\) W/m²·K⁴, the Stefan-Boltzmann constant.

Course outline

To give some idea of the course flow:

  • Weeks 1-3 will be about conduction: the conduction equation, the thermal resistance model, enhanced heat transfer via fins, and transient conduction.
  • Weeks 4-6 will be about convection: boundary layers, the significance of turbulence, flow over objects (external flow), flow through objects (internal flow), and heat exchangers.
  • Weeks 7-8 will be about radiation: the emissivity of real materials, the view factor, and the radiation resistance model.

Some vocabulary

Students should become familiar with these terms. (I will continue to add to the list as the course progresses.)

adiabatic
No heat transfer. Mathematically: at an adiabatic surface, \(dT/dx=0\) (from Fourier’s Law).
insulator
A material with low conductivity. A “perfect insulator” has \(k=0\), but a practical insulator like foam has \(k\approx 0.03\) W/m·K.
parameters and variables
Parameters are things that can adopt different values, but that tend to be kept constant within a problem. Examples are: material properties like density \(\rho\) and viscosity \(\mu\), sizes like length \(L\) and diameter \(D\), and usually the ambient temperature \(T_\infty\). Variables can adopt different values within an analysis, e.g., if we needed to determine the necessary wind speed \(U\) to keep an object below a certain temperature, \(U\) is a variable in that problem.
thermal conductivity
A material property that emerges from the empirical Fourier’s Law. SI units: (Watts)/(meter Kelvin). Note that the Kelvin here is related to a difference in temperature, and a \(\Delta T = 1~\text{K}\) is the same as a \(\Delta T = 1^\circ\text{C}\). That is, no unit transformation is necessary to go between W/m·K and W/m·°C.
thermal diffusivity
A material property defined as \(\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)\), with units m²/s. It describes how quickly a temperature disturbance propagates through a material. A high \(\alpha\) means the material conducts heat readily relative to how much it stores — temperature changes spread fast.
thermal inertia
The product \(\rho c\) (or \(mc\)), with units J/m³·K (or J/K). It describes resistance to temperature change — a large thermal inertia means a lot of energy must be added or removed to shift the temperature. A cast-iron pan has high thermal inertia; a thin aluminum sheet has low thermal inertia.
heat flux
Denoted \(q''\), heat flux is the rate of heat transfer per unit area, with units W/m². In general it is a vector quantity pointing in the direction of heat flow, though we also discuss it in one-dimensional problems. Contrast with \(\dot{Q}\) (total heat transfer rate, in W), which is a scalar quantity that describes the total heat transfer into or out of a system (potentially calculated by integrating the heat flux over the entirety of the surface).
convection coefficient
Also called the heat transfer coefficient, denoted \(h\), with units W/m²·K. It appears in Newton’s Law of Cooling: \(\dot{Q} = h A (T_\text{surface} - T_\infty)\). It is not a material property — it depends on the geometry, the fluid, and the flow conditions.
thermal resistance
Denoted \(R\), with units K/W. By analogy with Ohm’s Law (\(V = IR\)), thermal resistance relates a temperature difference to a heat transfer rate: \(\Delta T = \dot{Q}R\).
steady state vs. transient
A problem is steady state (or steady) if nothing changes with time: all \(\partial /\partial t = 0\). It is transient if something changes with time.
boundary conditions and initial conditions
Necessary to solve differential equations. Boundary conditions apply to spatial locations, e.g. at \(x=0\), \(T=4\)… that is a boundary condition. Initial conditions apply to points in time, usually \(t=0\). At \(t=0\), \(T=50\)… that is an initial condition.
Biot number
A dimensionless parameter defined as \(\text{Bi} = hL_c/k\), where \(L_c\) is a characteristic length, \(h\) is the convection coefficient, and \(k\) is the solid’s thermal conductivity.
