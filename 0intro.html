From thermodynamics, recall that the energy of a system is changed via heat transfer or work (on or by). The Laws of Thermodynamics say nothing about rates! Heat transfer is the study of the heat transfer rate.

We give the symbol \(\dot{Q}\) to the heat transfer rate, where \(Q\) is the amount of heat (energy), in Joules, and the dot on top references a time derivative.

Conduction

Primarily associated with solids, though fluids have a “thermal conductivity” too (convection is stronger).

\[\dot{Q}_\text{conduction} \sim k \cdot A \cdot \frac{T - T_0}{w}\]

Symbol Meaning Units
\(k\) thermal conductivity (material property) W/m·K
\(A\) cross-sectional area m²
\(T - T_0\) temperature difference between surfaces separated by width \(w\) K
\(w\) width (thickness) m

Convection

Medium is a fluid (gas or liquid), somehow correlated with speed.

\[\dot{Q}_\text{convection} \sim h \cdot A \cdot (T - T_0)\]

Symbol Meaning Units
\(h\) convective heat transfer coefficient (depends on material, size, speed, …) W/m²·K
\(A\) surface area m²
\(T\) object temperature K
\(T_0\) fluid temperature K

Radiation

Does not require a medium — transmitted by light/photons.

\[\dot{Q}_\text{radiation} \sim \varepsilon \cdot \sigma \cdot F \cdot A \cdot (T^4 - T_0^4)\]

Symbol Meaning Units
\(\varepsilon\) emissivity — how perfect is “the emitter” (material property, \(0 \leq \varepsilon \leq 1\)) —
\(\sigma\) Stefan-Boltzmann constant \(5.67 \times 10^{-8}\) W/m²·K⁴
\(F\) view factor — how well the objects “see” each other —
\(A\) surface area m²
\(T\) object surface temperature K
\(T_0\) surroundings temperature (e.g. \(T_\text{sun}\)) K

Course outline

To give some context for the course flow:

  • Weeks 1-3 will be about conduction: the conduction equation, the thermal resistance model, fins (one of the most important engineering design strategies), and transient conduction.
  • Weeks 4-6 will be about convection: boundary layers, the significance of turbulence, flow over objects (external flow), flow through objects (internal flow), and heat exchangers.
  • Weeks 7-8 will be about radiation: emissivity (a material property), the view factor, and the radiation resistance model.

Some vocabulary

adiabatic
No heat transfer. Mathematically (from Fourier’s Law), \(dT/dx\) at an adiabatic surface is zero.
insulator
A material with low conductivity. A “perfect insulator” has \(k=0\), but a practical insulator like foam has \(k\approx 0.03\)~W/m\(\cdot\)K. Air is a good insulator (indeed, foam is mostly air) with \(k=0.026\)~W/m\(\cdot\)K, but one must be careful to not induce natural convection (which would increase heat transfer).
parameters
Parameters are things that can adopt different values, but that tend to be kept constant within a problem. Examples are: material properties like density \(\rho\) and viscosity \(\mu\), sizes like length \(L\) and diameter \(D\), and usually surface \(T_\text{surf}\) or ambient \(T_\infty\) temperatures. Technically all of these are described by symbols, but the values tend to stay constant within a problem. We know we are using water, we know the incoming air is at a controlled temperature, etc.
variables
Variables can adopt different values within an analysis, e.g.: how much more heat transfer \(\dot{Q}\) will we get if we double the incoming flow speed \(U\)? Both \(\dot{Q}\) and \(U\) are variables in this problem. I might use a notation like \(\dot{Q}(U;\rho,\mu)\), with parameters after the ;, to make clear that the value of \(\dot{Q}\) technically depends on \(\rho\) and \(\mu\) but the primary variation we are examining is with respect to \(U\).
thermal conductivity
A material property that emerges from the empirical Fourier’s Law. SI units: (Watts)/(meter Kelvin). Note that the Kelvin here is related to a difference in temperature, and \(\Delta T = 1~\text{K} = 1^\circ\text{C}\). That is, no unit transformation is necessary to go between W/m\(\cdot\)K and W/m\(\cdot^\circ\) C.
thermal diffusivity
A material property defined as \(\alpha = k/(\rho c_p)\), with units m²/s. It describes how quickly a temperature disturbance propagates through a material. A high \(\alpha\) means the material conducts heat readily relative to how much it stores — temperature changes spread fast.
thermal inertia
The product \(\rho c\) (or \(mc\)), with units J/m³·K (or J/K). It describes resistance to temperature change — a large thermal inertia means a lot of energy must be added or removed to shift the temperature. A cast-iron pan has high thermal inertia; a thin aluminum sheet has low thermal inertia.
heat flux
Denoted \(q''\), heat flux is the rate of heat transfer per unit area, with units W/m². It is a vector quantity pointing in the direction of heat flow. Contrast with \(\dot{Q}\) (total heat transfer rate, in W), which is the flux integrated over an area: \(\dot{Q} = q'' A\).
convection coefficient
Also called the heat transfer coefficient, denoted \(h\), with units W/m²·K. It appears in Newton’s Law of Cooling: \(\dot{Q} = h A (T_\text{surface} - T_\infty)\). It is not a material property — it depends on the geometry, the fluid, and the flow conditions.
thermal resistance
Denoted \(R\), with units K/W. By analogy with Ohm’s Law (\(V = IR\)), thermal resistance relates a temperature difference to a heat transfer rate: \(\Delta T = \dot{Q}R\).
steady state vs. transient
A problem is steady state (or steady) if nothing changes with time: all \(\partial /\partial t = 0\). It is transient if something changes with time.
boundary conditions and initial conditions
Necessary to solve differential equations. Boundary conditions apply to spatial locations, e.g. at \(x=0\), \(T=4\). Initial conditions apply to points in time, usually \(t=0\).
Biot number
A dimensionless parameter defined as \(Bi = hL_c/k\), where \(L_c\) is a characteristic length, \(h\) is the convection coefficient, and \(k\) is the solid’s thermal conductivity.

## Some vocabulary

: No heat transfer. Mathematically (from Fourier's Law), $dT/dx$ at an adiabatic surface is zero.

insulator
: A material with low conductivity. A "perfect insulator" has $k=0$, but a practical insulator like foam has $k\approx 0.03$~W/m$\cdot$K. : Denoted $R$, with units K/W. By analogy with Ohm's Law ($V = IR$), thermal resistance relates a temperature difference to a heat transfer rate: $\Delta T =  \dot{Q}R$.

: A problem is **steady state** (or steady) if nothing changes with time: all $\partial /\partial t = 0$. It is **transient** if something changes with time.

boundary conditions and initial conditions
: Necessary to solve differential equations. Boundary conditions apply to spatial locations, e.g. at $x=0$, $T=4$. Initial conditions apply to points in time, usually $t=0$.

: A dimensionless parameter defined as $Bi = hL_c/k$, where $L_c$ is a characteristic length, $h$ is the convection coefficient, and $k$ is the solid's thermal conductivity.
