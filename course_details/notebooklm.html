NotebookLM Portfolio

Overview

NotebookLM is a free AI tool from Google that retrieves and synthesizes content from documents you upload. Unlike general AI tools like ChatGPT, it can only draw from sources you provide — it cannot generate answers from general knowledge. If the answer is not in your notes, NotebookLM cannot give it to you.

You will use NotebookLM throughout this course to organize and query your lecture notes. The NotebookLM portfolio is worth 10% of your course grade and is submitted at two checkpoints: Week 4 (formative feedback only) and Week 8 (graded).

Why NotebookLM

Three reasons this tool is part of the course:

First, it encourages better notes. Heat transfer is highly cumulative — material from Week 1 appears in problems in Week 6. A well-organized, searchable notebook makes that cumulative structure manageable rather than overwhelming.

Second, it supports engagement in an asynchronous course. NotebookLM is only as good as the notes you put into it. Weak notes produce a weak notebook. The portfolio submission is partly a check that you are actually watching lectures and engaging with the material.

Third, it models appropriate AI use. There is a meaningful difference between an AI tool that retrieves your own understanding and one that generates answers you do not have. NotebookLM cannot do your thinking for you — it can only help you access thinking you have already done. That is a useful distinction, and one that generalizes beyond this course.

How NotebookLM Works

  1. Create a free notebook at notebooklm.google.com using your CU Boulder account. (We get a slightly more robust version when we login using CU credentials.)
  2. Upload your lecture notes as sources: PDFs, Google Docs, or text files work well. If you want to talk about preparing your notes using Markdown or LaTeX, let me know and I can prepare a tutorial!
  3. Query your notebook using natural language: “What is the physical significance of the Biot number?” or “Show me my notes on fin efficiency”.
  4. NotebookLM synthesizes relevant content from your uploaded sources and cites which source it drew from.

Setup (Week 1)

There is a course video for Week 1 (~15 minutes) that walks you through NotebookLM.

Portfolio Submission

The portfolio has two components, submitted together at each checkpoint.

Query Log

A curated list of 10-15 queries you found most useful, with brief annotations. For each query:

  • The query you submitted
  • A 1-2 sentence annotation explaining why this query was useful — what it helped you retrieve, clarify, or connect

The goal is not a comprehensive log of every query you ran. It is a curated selection that demonstrates you used the tool in a way that supported your learning.

Example of a useful annotated entry:

Query: “What assumptions are required for the lumped capacitance method?”

Annotation: This came up when I was working on Report 2 and couldn’t remember the Biot number criterion. The notebook pulled my notes from the Week 3 lecture and reminded me that Bi < 0.1 is the standard threshold, which I had written down but forgotten. Confirmed my approach was valid.

Example of a less useful annotated entry:

Query: “convection”

Annotation: I looked this up to review convection.

The first example shows genuine engagement. The second does not.

Reflection

For each major course unit, 1-2 paragraphs addressing:

  • What did you search for that your notebook could not answer — and why not?
  • How did you resolve the gap? (Rewatched a lecture, worked a problem, asked during office hours, found it in the textbook?)

This section is more valuable than the query log in some ways. The gaps in your notebook are a record of where your notes were thin, where a concept didn’t stick, or where you needed a source your notebook didn’t have. Reflecting on how you resolved those gaps is exactly the kind of metacognitive habit that leads to a greater understanding… of your understanding!

Grading

This will largely be graded on completion and genuine engagement, not on the sophistication of your notes or of your queries. A notebook built from genuine note-taking with thoughtful queries should earn full marks.

What earns less than full marks: query logs with vague annotations that do not explain what was useful, or reflections that describe no gaps (which suggests either the notebook is not being used or the reflections are not genuine).

Bringing Your Notebook to Oral Exams

You are strongly encouraged to bring your NotebookLM to both oral exams. You may query it during the exam.

This is an advantage — but only if you built it well. Students with rich, well-organized notebooks find it genuinely useful under pressure. Students who uploaded thin notes find that querying returns thin results at an inconvenient moment.

The oral exam is, among other things, an authenticity check on the portfolio. A student who built a strong notebook uses it fluently. A student who did not built one finds quickly that there is nothing useful to retrieve.

Some ways of thinking about NotebookLM

Maybe one of these will speak to you?

  1. NotebookLM can be thought of as an “external brain” that contains the details, while you focus on the engineering process. You know there is a correlation for the Nusselt Number for a particular geometry, and instead of digging through your notes to find it, you prompt your notebook and get started writing out the problem while it thinks. In the 15 seconds it takes to retrieve the relevant equation, you have already written out the assumptions and first step of the solution and are ready to plug-in the equation.
  2. Similarly: you can think of yourself as a senior engineer, and NotebookLM is a junior engineer, and you are working on a project together. Don’t blame the junior engineer if there are bogus results, you trained them!
  3. NotebookLM is a way to check that a human colleague (or maybe you in six months) could get up-to-speed on a project based on your notes. If the AI can’t read your work, or is confused by your organization, or makes some incorrect connections, a human would probably do the same. If your notebook is good, a person could use it to pick up a project easily. Future you will thank you.

Some words on note-taking

This aspect of the course is a bit peculiar, particularly for an upper-level course… you get points for taking notes? But you take notes in other classes and don’t get points? We are emphasizing “knowledge management” in this course because it is a skill that is going to become very important to you very soon, with senior design and your career on the horizon. In industry, if your work isn’t documented well, it didn’t happen.

Some students are already taking clean detailed notes - for them, cultivating their notebook will be easier, and they will be less likely to encounter a confused AI… is that an m, or an n? But this isn’t that different from a student who has a stronger background in differential equations: that student will likely have an easier time with the math in this course. Both technical ability and the ability to communicate are being emphasized in this course.

AI Policy

NotebookLM is indeed an AI tool, though I believe it is an example of “using AI the right way”. There are right ways to use any AI, but the trouble with LLMs, at least in an educational context, is that they are too eager to just do the work for you. “How can I factor a heat generation rate of \(5x(1-x)\) into my conduction equation?” gets an immediate response: “Here is the solution, and here is the maximum temperature, and here is the heat flux out each wall. Is there anything else I can help you with?” That is not serving your development as an engineer. Officially, general AI use is prohibited in this class, though I am not naive enough to think that policing it is a good use of my time.

There are probably wrong ways to use NotebookLM as well. As we saw in the tutorial video, you can push NotebookLM to do a little math for you. I’m not sure how far this can go… I guess you can explore, but make sure you are the one doing your homework.

The best way to learn anything is to struggle through it, grappling with uncertainty, finding and correcting mistakes, checking with intuition along they way. NotebookLM will allow you to still do those things, and thus still learn, while also reducing the tedium of digging through your notes.
