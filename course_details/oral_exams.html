Oral Exams

Overview

There are two oral exams in this course: a midterm oral (~Week 4) and a final oral (~Week 7-8). Together with the capstone report, they account for 52% of your course grade.

Oral exams are uncommon in undergraduate engineering programs. This page explains how they work, what is expected, and how to prepare.

Why Oral Exams

The short answer: they are the most direct window into understanding that exists in a course setting.

A written solution can be produced without understanding the reasoning behind it. A conversation cannot. When you are asked to explain why you made a modeling choice, what would change if a parameter shifted, or where your solution would break down — the answer either comes from genuine understanding or it does not. There is no middle ground.

This is not about catching anyone out. It is about making the grade mean something. A student who earns a strong grade in this course has demonstrated, in real time, that they can reason about heat transfer. That is a credential worth having.

Format

Both oral exams follow the same three-part structure:

Part 1: Anchor Question (~4-5 minutes)

A conceptual question, the same for every student, released one week before the exam. You will know exactly what this question is before you sit down.

Example: “Walk me through transient conduction in a semi-infinite solid — what is happening physically, what are the key parameters, and what does the solution tell you?”

The anchor question is released in advance to reduce anxiety and reward preparation, not to make the exam easy. A strong answer demonstrates organized conceptual understanding. A weak answer — even on a question you had a week to prepare — tells us something important.

Part 2: Report Defense (~5-7 minutes)

The examiner will draw from one of your submitted technical reports. You will be asked to walk through your reasoning, justify your assumptions, and explain your sanity checks.

Expect questions like:

  • “You wrote X on page 2 — walk me through that reasoning.”
  • “You assumed Y here. What would change if that assumption didn’t hold?”
  • “Your pitfall section says you initially got the boundary condition wrong. How did you catch that?”

The report you defend will be drawn from your submitted work up to that point. For the midterm, this means reports submitted through approximately Week 3. You will not be told in advance which report will be discussed.

Part 3: Extension Question (~3-5 minutes)

A “what if” question that modifies a parameter or removes an assumption from the problem you just discussed.

Example: “You found the steady-state temperature distribution assuming constant thermal conductivity. What would change if conductivity varied with temperature?”

This part of the exam cannot be prepared for by memorizing solutions. It can only be prepared for by understanding the physics well enough to reason about perturbations.

Total time: approximately 12-17 minutes per student.

Midterm Oral Exam (~Week 4)

Weight: 20% of course grade

The midterm oral is primarily formative — it is a calibration point that tells you where you stand while there is still time to improve. A weak midterm performance is recoverable.

You may bring your NotebookLM notebook to the exam.

Retake Policy

A retake of the midterm oral is available to any student who wants one.

How it works:

  • The retake score replaces the original score — for better or worse. If you do better, your grade improves. If you do worse, your grade drops. This is intentional: the retake is for students who are genuinely better prepared, not for students who are hoping for an easier question.
  • The retake uses a different anchor question and draws from a wider report pool. It is designed to be comparable in difficulty, not easier.
  • To schedule a retake, you must first submit a short written reflection (see below).

Retake reflection (required to schedule):

Submit a paragraph — 3-5 sentences — addressing the following:

  1. What specific concept or question in the exam did you struggle with, and why?
  2. What have you done since the exam to address that gap?
  3. What are you prepared to demonstrate in the retake that you could not demonstrate the first time?

Vague reflections (“I didn’t study enough”) will be returned without scheduling a retake. Specific reflections (“I couldn’t explain why the Biot number assumption broke down in the composite wall problem, so I reworked problems 2 and 4 from the Tier 1 set”) will be scheduled promptly.

The reflection is not graded. It is a commitment device — it filters for students who have actually done additional preparation, and it gives the examiner useful information going into the retake.

Final Oral Exam (~Week 7-8)

Weight: Approximately 22% of course grade (70% of the combined 32% capstone + final oral weight)

The final oral is high-stakes. It interrogates your capstone report deeply and tests your ability to synthesize across the full course.

Structure

Time Content
0-3 min Student gives a 2-minute unprompted summary of their capstone system and findings
3-10 min Examiner probes mechanism identification and modeling choices from capstone
10-16 min “What if” questions — change a parameter, remove an assumption, add a mode
16-20 min Student reflection — what would you do differently, what are you least confident in

The final oral differs from the midterm in one important way: the last four minutes are yours. You are asked to identify the weakest part of your own work. This is not a trap — an engineer who can articulate the limitations of their own analysis is more valuable than one who cannot.

You may bring your NotebookLM notebook to the exam.

How to Prepare

The most effective preparation is doing the work throughout the course. Students who engage genuinely with Tier 1 problems, write their own technical reports, and participate actively in peer discussions consistently find oral exams manageable. Students who do not find them very uncomfortable.

Beyond that:

  • Practice explaining out loud. Writing and speaking are different cognitive acts. Talk through your solutions to a roommate, a study group, or yourself.
  • Know your reports. You wrote them — you should be able to walk through any part of them without looking at the document.
  • Prepare your anchor question seriously. You have a week. A well-organized, physically motivated answer to the anchor question sets a strong tone for everything that follows.
  • Build your NotebookLM. Students who have rich, well-organized notebooks use them fluently in the exam. Students who built them poorly find them unhelpful under pressure.

Logistics

Exams are conducted by the instructor and TAs via Zoom. Scheduling windows will be posted in Canvas approximately two weeks before each exam period. Sign up early — late slots fill last and create unnecessary deadline pressure.

Each exam is approximately 12-17 minutes. Plan for 20 minutes including brief setup and transition time.
