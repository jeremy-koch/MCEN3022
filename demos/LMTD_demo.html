Log-Mean Temperature Difference — Interactive Demo

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

When a fluid flows through a heat exchanger, the temperature difference between the hot and cold streams changes along the length. The local driving force for heat transfer is the local \(\Delta T\), so we can’t just use the inlet or outlet difference as the “effective” average — we need the log-mean temperature difference:

\[\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\Delta T_1 - \Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)}\]

where \(\Delta T_1\) and \(\Delta T_2\) are the temperature differences at the two ends of the exchanger (hot-inlet end and hot-outlet end, respectively).

The temperature profiles along the exchanger are exponential — not linear — determined by the heat capacity rates \(C = \dot{m}c_p\) of each stream. Given the four terminal temperatures, those profiles are fully determined, and that’s what the demo plots.

What to look for

Counter flow outperforms parallel flow — with the same terminal temperatures. Switch between the two arrangements with the defaults: \(T_h\): 90 → 60 °C, \(T_c\): 20 → 55 °C. The arithmetic mean \(\Delta T\) is the same for both (it only depends on the four terminal values). But \(\Delta T_{lm}\) is notably higher for counter flow. That gap is the engineering motivation for counter-flow designs: same heat exchanged, smaller required area — or equivalently, better fluid temperatures for the same hardware.

The visual explanation is on the plot: parallel flow forces both \(\Delta T_1\) and \(\Delta T_2\) to be skewed (one large, one tiny), while counter flow keeps them more balanced. The log-mean penalizes large imbalance.

The isothermal stream. Check “Isothermal” on the hot side and observe: the red line flattens, and the cold stream curves exponentially toward it. This is the tube-bank situation from ?sec-external-flow-tube-bank: the tube surface is at constant \(T_s\), and the external flow heats up as it passes through — following exactly \(T_c(\xi) = T_s - (T_s - T_{c,\text{in}})\,e^{-R\xi}\).

Note

An isothermal stream doesn’t have to mean phase change (condensation, evaporation). It also approximates any stream with a very large capacity rate \(C = \dot{m}c_p\): if enough fluid is flowing, its temperature barely shifts, and it looks flat on the plot. Check “Isothermal” on one side, then imagine it as the limit of sliding that side’s outlet temperature back toward its inlet.

Arithmetic mean always overestimates. The summary panel shows the arithmetic mean alongside \(\Delta T_{lm}\). The arithmetic mean is always \(\geq \Delta T_{lm}\) (by the AM–GM inequality applied to the exponential profile). Using it in \(\dot{Q} = \overline{h}A\,\Delta T\) would overestimate the heat transfer rate. The correction matters most when \(\Delta T_1 / \Delta T_2\) is far from 1 — as in the parallel-flow default, where it is off by roughly 50%.

When \(\Delta T_1 = \Delta T_2\), \(\Delta T_{lm} = \Delta T_1\) exactly (L’Hôpital’s rule on the \(0/0\) form). The profiles become straight lines and the arithmetic mean is correct. This happens in counter flow when the two streams have equal capacity rates — a balanced, perfectly efficient exchanger.

Worked example — tube bank as isothermal hot surface

Return to the tube bank from the chapter. Air at \(T_i = 15\,^\circ\)C passes through a bank of tubes held at \(T_s = 70\,^\circ\)C. The outlet temperature predicted by the correlation is \(T_o = 35\,^\circ\)C. Find \(\Delta T_{lm}\) and the total heat transfer.

Step 1 — Identify the temperature differences. The hot surface is isothermal at \(T_s = 70\,^\circ\)C, so:

\[\Delta T_1 = T_s - T_i = 70 - 15 = 55\,^\circ\text{C}, \qquad \Delta T_2 = T_s - T_o = 70 - 35 = 35\,^\circ\text{C}\]

Step 2 — Apply the log-mean formula.

\[\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{55 - 35}{\ln(55/35)} = \frac{20}{\ln 1.571} = \frac{20}{0.452} = 44.3\,^\circ\text{C}\]

Check against the demo: set Hot isothermal at 70°C, \(T_{c,\text{in}} = 15\)°C, \(T_{c,\text{out}} = 35\)°C. The summary should read \(\Delta T_{lm} \approx 44.3\,^\circ\)C.

The arithmetic mean would give \((55 + 35)/2 = 45\,^\circ\)C — only about 1.6% high here because \(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2 = 1.57\) is close to 1. At larger ratios, the error grows fast.

Step 3 — Total heat transfer (with \(N = 80\) tubes, \(D = 0.025\) m, \(L = 1\) m, \(\overline{h} = 95\) W/m²·K):

\[\dot{Q} = N\,\overline{h}\,\pi D L\,\Delta T_{lm} = 80 \times 95 \times \pi \times 0.025 \times 1 \times 44.3 \approx 26.5\;\text{kW}\]

# Log-Mean Temperature Difference — Interactive Demo {.unnumbered}

When a fluid flows through a heat exchanger, the temperature difference between the hot and cold streams changes along the length. The local driving force for heat transfer is the local $\Delta T$, so we can't just use the inlet or outlet difference as the "effective" average — we need the **log-mean temperature difference**:

$$\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{\Delta T_1 - \Delta T_2}{\ln(\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2)}$$

where $\Delta T_1$ and $\Delta T_2$ are the temperature differences at the two ends of the exchanger (hot-inlet end and hot-outlet end, respectively).

The temperature *profiles* along the exchanger are exponential — not linear — determined by the heat capacity rates $C = \dot{m}c_p$ of each stream. Given the four terminal temperatures, those profiles are fully determined, and that's what the demo plots.

## What to look for {#sec-observations}

**Counter flow outperforms parallel flow — with the same terminal temperatures.**
Switch between the two arrangements with the defaults: $T_h$: 90 → 60 °C, $T_c$: 20 → 55 °C. The arithmetic mean $\Delta T$ is the same for both (it only depends on the four terminal values). But $\Delta T_{lm}$ is notably higher for counter flow. That gap is the engineering motivation for counter-flow designs: same heat exchanged, smaller required area — or equivalently, better fluid temperatures for the same hardware.

The visual explanation is on the plot: parallel flow forces both $\Delta T_1$ and $\Delta T_2$ to be skewed (one large, one tiny), while counter flow keeps them more balanced. The log-mean penalizes large imbalance.

**The isothermal stream.** Check "Isothermal" on the hot side and observe: the red line flattens, and the cold stream curves exponentially toward it. This is the tube-bank situation from @sec-external-flow-tube-bank: the tube surface is at constant $T_s$, and the external flow heats up as it passes through — following exactly $T_c(\xi) = T_s - (T_s - T_{c,\text{in}})\,e^{-R\xi}$.

::: {.callout-note}
An isothermal stream doesn't have to mean phase change (condensation, evaporation). It also approximates any stream with a very large capacity rate $C = \dot{m}c_p$: if enough fluid is flowing, its temperature barely shifts, and it looks flat on the plot. Check "Isothermal" on one side, then imagine it as the limit of sliding that side's outlet temperature back toward its inlet.
:::

**Arithmetic mean always overestimates.** The summary panel shows the arithmetic mean alongside $\Delta T_{lm}$. The arithmetic mean is always $\geq \Delta T_{lm}$ (by the AM–GM inequality applied to the exponential profile). Using it in $\dot{Q} = \overline{h}A\,\Delta T$ would overestimate the heat transfer rate. The correction matters most when $\Delta T_1 / \Delta T_2$ is far from 1 — as in the parallel-flow default, where it is off by roughly 50%.

**When $\Delta T_1 = \Delta T_2$**, $\Delta T_{lm} = \Delta T_1$ exactly (L'Hôpital's rule on the $0/0$ form). The profiles become straight lines and the arithmetic mean is correct. This happens in counter flow when the two streams have equal capacity rates — a balanced, perfectly efficient exchanger.

## Worked example — tube bank as isothermal hot surface {#sec-worked}

Return to the tube bank from the chapter. Air at $T_i = 15\,^\circ$C passes through a bank of tubes held at $T_s = 70\,^\circ$C. The outlet temperature predicted by the correlation is $T_o = 35\,^\circ$C. Find $\Delta T_{lm}$ and the total heat transfer.

**Step 1 — Identify the temperature differences.**
The hot surface is isothermal at $T_s = 70\,^\circ$C, so:

$$\Delta T_1 = T_s - T_i = 70 - 15 = 55\,^\circ\text{C}, \qquad \Delta T_2 = T_s - T_o = 70 - 35 = 35\,^\circ\text{C}$$

**Step 2 — Apply the log-mean formula.**

$$\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{55 - 35}{\ln(55/35)} = \frac{20}{\ln 1.571} = \frac{20}{0.452} = 44.3\,^\circ\text{C}$$

Check against the demo: set Hot isothermal at 70°C, $T_{c,\text{in}} = 15$°C, $T_{c,\text{out}} = 35$°C. The summary should read $\Delta T_{lm} \approx 44.3\,^\circ$C.

The arithmetic mean would give $(55 + 35)/2 = 45\,^\circ$C — only about 1.6% high here because $\Delta T_1/\Delta T_2 = 1.57$ is close to 1. At larger ratios, the error grows fast.

**Step 3 — Total heat transfer** (with $N = 80$ tubes, $D = 0.025$ m, $L = 1$ m, $\overline{h} = 95$ W/m²·K):

$$\dot{Q} = N\,\overline{h}\,\pi D L\,\Delta T_{lm} = 80 \times 95 \times \pi \times 0.025 \times 1 \times 44.3 \approx 26.5\;\text{kW}$$
