3  Transient

This week we are going to focus on transient conduction. In all cases, a solid object will be losing/gaining energy due to convective heat transfer. We are going to be thinking about temperature as a function of position AND time. That means our governing equations are PDEs! … But actually we’ll arrive at a few practical equations that will be useful in solving problems.

This section is big on thinking about the assumptions in the problems. Our first model, the Lumped Capacitance Model, will allow us to assume that there is no spatial-variation in the temperature within the object (which dramatically simplifies the analysis). The next step up in complication, the One-Term Approximation, will let us think about spatial variation with certain limitations. The full solution has infinitely many terms in it… we try to avoid using that one!

We’ll also think about the choice of boundary conditions: is it reasonable to say that the surface has a known temperature, or a known heat flux, or a known convection coefficient? There are differences in the solutions between these cases!

3.1 Think First

Recall

  1. What is thermal diffusivity, \(\alpha\)? What does a large \(\alpha\) mean physically – does heat move through the material quickly or slowly?
  2. From the resistor analogy: What is the thermal resistance for convection? For conduction?
  3. From circuits: What is the RC time constant? What determines how quickly a capacitor discharges, and what does the voltage look like as a function of time?

Look Ahead

  1. Under what conditions can we treat a solid object as having a single, uniform temperature, rather than a temperature distribution?
  2. How does the time it takes to cool an object depend on its size, material, and the surrounding fluid?
  3. If the ground heats up in summer, how far down does that temperature variation penetrate before winter?

3.2 The Lumped Capacitance Model

TipVideo

Start with this video link, which covers the Biot Number and Lumped Capacitance Model. Then read the following.

In engineering (and elsewhere), we sometimes talk about “the limiting factor” or “the bottleneck”. Some assorted examples:

  • There may be consumer demand for a new product, plenty of financial backing from investors, but the product is not taking off – its limiting factor is the manufacturing time.
  • Combustion is a chemical reaction that requires fuel, oxygen, and heat. If you spill some gasoline on the ground, there is plenty of fuel and oxygen available – the limiting factor to the chemical reaction is heat. (Be careful though, combustion creates its own energy once it gets going!)
  • In space travel, a returning spacecraft might have the right trajectory, the right parachutes, and a well-designed landing zone, but the limiting factor is heat. The vehicle is moving so fast that aerodynamic compression heats the surrounding air to thousands of degrees; the mission succeeds or fails entirely based on whether the heat shield can manage that thermal load.

The present discussion: we may have a situation in which a hot object needs to be cooled by a fluid passing over it. That process involves conduction of heat through the object (i.e., getting energy from its interior to its exterior) and convection out of the object (i.e., getting energy from the surface into the fluid). The “lumped capacitance model” assumes that the limiting factor of this process is the convection step.

3.2.1 The Biot Number

Another way to talk about this limiting factor: the thermal resistance for conduction is much less than that for convection. Framed in terms of the resistor model: \(R_\text{conduction} \ll R_\text{convection}\). Plugging-in the equations for these resistors

\[ \frac{L}{kA} \ll \frac{1}{hA} \implies \frac{hL}{k} \ll 1. \]

The quantity \(hL/k\) is a dimensionless number known as “The Biot Number”

\[ \text{Bi}\equiv \frac{hL_c}{k} \]

and in general it is expressed in terms of the convective heat transfer coefficient \(h\), the thermal conductivity of the solid object \(k\), and a “characteristic length” \(L_c\) that depends on the problem’s geometry. Whenever \(\text{Bi} \ll 1\), convection will be our limiting factor. In practice we say when \(\text{Bi} \leq 0.1\).

Caution

We will define another dimensionless number when studying convection, the Nusselt Number, that has the same definition as the Biot Number. The difference is: the Nusselt Number uses the thermal conductivity of the fluid, the Biot Number uses the thermal conductivity of the solid.

A generally applicable definition of the characteristic length is \(L_c \equiv (\text{volume})/(\text{surface area})\), but often we just choose a convenient lengthscale in the problem. E.g., for a sphere, some folks will just use the sphere radius.

An implication of this observation is that the temperature within an object will have time to equilibrate with itself: any initial gradients in temperature will lead to heat flow from hot to cold until the object is approximately at uniform temperature. We can then say the object has one temperature (rather than a temperature distribution), and can analyze how that one temperature changes with time. This is the “lumped capacitance model/assumption”.

Why “lumped capacitance”? “Lumping” means to disregard differences (in this case variation in temperature within an object) and “capacitance” is (probably) related to the idea that we are discharging voltage (temperature) within the object, similar to what happens in a capacitor circuit. I’ve never really written out an explanation before…

3.2.2 Problem analysis

We are going to imagine a solid object – say a sphere or cylinder – with known physical properties and geometry; and flow over that object, with known convective heat transfer coefficient \(h\) and temperature \(T_\infty\). A very practical question: if the object starts at a temperature \(T_i\), how long does it take for the center of the object to reach \(T_\text{cool}\)?

Need more motivation? You just made a guaranteed-delicious cookie. It comes out of the oven too hot to eat – how long do you have to wait until it hits that perfect temperature where the chocolate is melty, but you won’t burn yourself?

With the lumped capacitance assumption, we can write an energy balance for the object, assuming that the energy of the object is changing only due to convection:

\[ \frac{dE}{dt}=mc\frac{dT}{dt} = -hA(T - T_\infty). \]

We will get better physical insight when we go through a non-dimensionalization process. If you are interested in the mathematical details, they’ll be in the collapsed menu below.

NoteClick here to see the details of the non-dimensionalization.

We’ll use a definition of dimensionless \(\theta\) that is slightly different from the one we used with the fins

\[ \theta \equiv \frac{T - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} \]

and we will speculate that there is a “characteristic time” in the problem, \(t_c\), to-be-determined. Then we can write a dimensionless time

\[ \tau \equiv \frac{t}{t_c}. \]

Use the chain rule:

\[ \frac{d}{dt} = \frac{d\tau}{dt}\frac{d}{d\tau} = \frac{1}{t_c}\frac{d}{d\tau} \]

and a little bit of calculus:

\[ \frac{dT}{d\tau}=\frac{d}{d\tau}\left(T_\infty + (T_i-T_\infty)\theta\right) = (T_i-T_\infty)\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}. \]

Plugging-in to our energy balance \[ mc\frac{(T_i-T_\infty)}{t_c}\frac{dT}{d\tau} = -hA(T_\infty + ((T_i-T_\infty)\theta-T_\infty) \]

and doing some rearranging, we land at

\[ \frac{1}{t_c}\frac{d\theta}{d\tau} = -\frac{hA}{mc} \theta. \]

Now, we haven’t defined \(t_c\) yet. Notice that \(\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}\) and \(\theta\) are dimensionless… that means \(mc/(hA)\) must have dimensions of time. For mathematical convenience and for physical insight, we can define \(t_c = mc/(hA)\) as our characteristic time.

We land on a differential equation, expressed in dimensionless form:

\[ \frac{d\theta}{d\tau}=-\theta \]

where

\[ \theta \equiv \frac{T - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} \]

and

\[ \tau \equiv \frac{t}{t_c}\quad\text{with }t_c \equiv \frac{mc}{hA}. \]

The differential equation is solved by recognizing the initial condition is \(\theta(0)=1\):

\[ \theta(\tau) = \exp(-\tau). \tag{3.1}\]

Or, converted back into a dimensional equation:

\[ T(t) = T_\infty + (T_i - T_\infty)\exp\left(-\frac{hA}{mc}t\right). \tag{3.2}\]

3.2.2.1 Some observations

  1. This analysis reveals that, as long as \(\text{Bi}\leq 0.1\), the cooling-off process is the same for all materials: given by Equation 3.1, an exponential decay. Dimensionally, Equation 3.2, the decay is stretched/shrunk by \(t_c\), and the initial and final temperature values depend on \(T_i\) and \(T_\infty\). But all the possible variations in the parameters collapse onto the single dimensionless solution, Equation 3.1.

  2. Are the factors in the characteristic time intuitive? The definition is \(t_c = mc/(hA)\), and it appears in the equation as \(\exp(-hAt/(mc))\). A larger value of \(h\) means better cooling, and so the temperature should decrease faster… indeed, \(h\) appears upstairs in the exponential. A larger object, meaning larger mass \(m\), will cool slower… it makes sense that \(m\) is downstairs. What about the other factors?

  3. The characteristic time is sometimes called “the time constant”, and this language is used in many fields (e.g., you might recall it from your circuits class). In truth, it takes infinite time for the object to reach \(T_\infty\): the decaying exponential asymptotes to \(T_\infty\), but never gets there. However, it is not productive to say “it takes literally forever to cool down”. Instead we reference the time constant. Develop this common language: after one time constant, the process is 63% of the way towards steady state. After two time constants, it is 86% of the way there. And we might say “its time constant is \(10\times\) as high”, which practically means it takes \(10\times\) as long to cool.

3.3.1 Series Solution

For a sphere, the conduction equation can be simplified to

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}= \alpha \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2 \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right). \]

The assumptions are mostly standard, think through how we’d get here. One is worth a bit of discussion: we are going to assume that an average \(h\) is valid for the entire sphere. Actually the “local” value of \(h\) – that is, the value of \(h\) at a particular location on the surface – is going to depend on the location on the surface. Think about boundary layers, flow separation, turbulent wakes, etc. … we’re not going to assume those things don’t exist, but we are going to claim we can average all that behavior into a single value of \(h\).

What are the initial/boundary conditions? Because the PDE is first-order in time, we need one initial condition; and because it is second order in \(r\), we need two boundary conditions. They are:

  • We know the initial temperature distribution is uniform: \(T(r,0) = T_i\).
  • We know the solution has to be physically realistic, so \(T(r,t)\) is bounded. (Where does this come up? We might see that solving the PDE leads to a term in the general solution \(\sim a_1/r\), which goes to infinity at \(r=0\). To keep that from happening, we pick \(a_1=0\).)
  • We know that the heat arriving at the surface via conduction leaves the surface due to convection: \(-k\frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\bigg\rvert_R = h(T(R)-T_\infty)\).

With these, the problem is solvable after a lot of math: \[ \theta(r,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_n \exp\!\left(-\zeta_n^2 \cdot \text{Fo}\right) \frac{\sin\!\left(\zeta_n \cdot \frac{r}{R}\right)}{\zeta_n r/R} \tag{3.3}\]

where \(\text{Fo} \equiv \alpha t/R^2\) is known as “The Fourier Number”, which can be thought of as a dimensionless time.

The Fourier Number is basically a measure of the progression of heat through an object: if time is large, or the thermal diffusivity is large, or the object is small, the Fourier Number is big.

The “eigenvalues” of the problem, \(\zeta_n\), are the solutions to the equation

\[ 1 - \zeta_n \cot \zeta_n = \text{Bi}. \]

(If you are interested in the details you can read up on the “separation of variables” technique for solving PDEs – \(\zeta_n\) are special values that connect the time- and space-dependent aspects of the solution.) We also have coefficients

\[ C_n = \frac{4\left[\sin\zeta_n - \zeta_n\cos\zeta_n\right]}{2\zeta_n - \sin(2\zeta_n)}. \]

So: if we have calculated our Biot Number, we can calculate our \(\zeta_n\) values (there are infinitely many of them), and then our coefficient \(C_n\) values (infinitely many). Then we can compute the infinite summation to determine the temperature at any radial location, at any time.

Calculating infinity terms? Hopefully there is a better way…

3.3.2 The One-Term Approximation

The eigenvalues of the problem are calculated in ascending order: $_1 < _2 < _3 < … $. For the sphere problem, the eigenvalues are about \(\pi\) apart, and that is more-or-less the case for any geometry. What does that mean for the decaying exponential term in Equation 3.3? The \(n=2\) term is going to be much much smaller than the \(n=1\) term (assuming \(\text{Fo}\) is not too close to zero).

With this observation, we make the “one-term approximation”: due to that decaying exponential, we neglect the \(n=2\), \(n=3\), … , \(n=1000\), … terms from the summation: only the \(n=1\) substantially contributes. Then

\[ \theta = C_1 \exp\!\left(-\zeta_1^2 \cdot \text{Fo}\right) \frac{\sin\!\left(\zeta_1 \cdot \frac{r}{R}\right)}{\zeta_1 r/R} \]

and, after unpacking, we understand our temperature distribution \(T(r,t)\). We typically say this equation is valid and useful when \(\text{Bi}>0.1\) and \(\text{Fo}>0.2\).

A note on \(\text{Fo}>0.2\): A typical problem we’d hope to solve is “find the time when…”. Even in the case where we don’t know what that time is a priori (ahead of time), we can make the assumption that the one-term approximation is valid, do the calculation, and then check that indeed \(\text{Fo}>0.2\). In any practical problem, we are not interested in an abrupt event: the Fourier Number is going to be plenty large for the one-term approximation.

3.5.1 The Semi-Infinite Solid

Our geometry for all cases is a “semi-infinite solid” where, for notational simplicity, we will use \(x\) as our vertical depth coordinate.

The depth spans from \(x=0\) to \(\infty\), and the idea is: we are adding heat at the surface (by four different methods), how does the temperature in the initially cold soil change with time? We will solve for \(T(x,t)\).

The starting point for all heat-addition methods is the conduction equation with no heat generation:

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=\alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}. \]

A PDE that is first-order in time and second-order in position, and so we’ll need one initial condition and two boundary conditions. For all the cases described below, we’ll use

\[ T(x,0)=T_i \]

… the initial condition that all of the soil is at a uniform temperature \(T_i\) at \(t=0\). We’ll also use

\[ T(\infty,t)=T_i \]

… the boundary condition that, at a depth of infinity, the temperature is at the initial temperature \(T_i\). The second boundary condition will vary problem-to-problem.

3.5.2 Case I: Known surface temperature \(T_s\)

We will assume that the surface temperature is at a known value such that our second boundary condition is

\[ \text{BC1:} \quad T(0,t) = T_s. \]

I’ll go through the derivation in this collapsable note, if you are interested.

NoteClick here to see the details of the derivation.

The solution idea: the heat addition at the surface is going to be felt at a greater depth \(x\) as time \(t\) increases, and so we are going to try to couple those variables together. (If you are interested in looking up another PDE solution technique, this one is called the similarity solution method.)

There is a way to systematically determine the way that the variables should be coupled, but I’m just going to skip that and say: let’s try this dimensionless variable

\[ \eta = \frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}. \]

If this all works out, we will arrive at a solution \(T(\eta)\), and we can plug-in the definition of \(\eta\) to get \(T(x,t)\).

Let’s again use a \(\theta\) (a non-dimensional temperature), defined as

\[ \theta \equiv \frac{T - T_s}{T_i - T_s} \]

Then

\[ \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} \]

becomes:

\[ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2} \tag{3.4}\]

Some chain rule calculations:

\[ \frac{\partial\theta}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} \]

\[ \frac{\partial^2\theta}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2} = \frac{1}{4\alpha t}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2} \]

\[ \frac{\partial\theta}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial t}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}}\cdot\frac{1}{t}\cdot\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} \]

Substituting into Equation 3.4 yields

\[ \frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}\,t}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \alpha\cdot\frac{1}{4\alpha t}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2}. \]

With some algebra, we can show that \(\frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}\,t}\) equals \(-\frac{1}{2}\eta\), and so we have

\[ -2\eta\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2} \]

…an ODE! Secretly, pretty much all of the PDE solution techniques involve turning the equation into an ODE (or a few ODEs) because we are comfortable solving ODEs. Importantly for this technique: we have arrived at a dimensionless equation that does not explicitly depend on \(x\) or \(t\), it instead is a function of the \(\eta\) we defined. This means our assumption of the existence of a coupled variable \(\eta\) was a good one.

We turned the equation into an ODE for \(\theta(\eta)\), so we must make our initial and boundary conditions apply to \(\theta(\eta)\). Examining our definitions for \(\theta\) and \(\eta\), we recognize that the initial condition becomes

\[ \quad T(x,0) = T_i \implies \theta(\infty) = 1, \]

the first boundary condition becomes

\[ T(\infty,t) = T_i \implies \theta(\infty) = 1 \]

(same as the initial condition, an important check to make sure this method works), and the second boundary condition becomes

\[ T(0,t) = T_s \implies \theta(0) = 0. \]

We’ll solve by integration… it might be clearer to define \(M(\eta)\equiv d\theta/d\eta\). Then

\[ \frac{dM}{d\eta} = -2\eta M \quad \Rightarrow \quad \int\frac{dM}{M} = \int -2\eta\,d\eta \]

which means

\[ \ln M = -\eta^2 + c_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad M = d_1\exp(-\eta^2) \]

that is:

\[ \frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = d_1\exp(-\eta^2). \]

Then we simply need to do another integration… the trouble is, the right-hand-side of this equation has no nice antiderivative. We can, and do, write

\[ \theta(\eta) = d_1\int_0^{\eta}\exp(-z^2)\,dz + d_2 \]

where \(z\) is a dummy variable. Applying the boundary/initial conditions:

\[ \theta(0) = 0 = d_1\int_0^0\cdots\,dz + d_2 \;\Rightarrow\; d_2 = 0 \]

and

\[ \theta(\infty) = 1 = d_1\int_0^{\infty}\exp(-z^2)\,dz = d_1 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}. \]

We arrive at our solution:

\[ \theta(\eta) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^{\eta}\exp(-z^2)\,dz. \]

This function is common enough that it has a name: “The Error Function”. We sometimes denote it \(\theta(\eta)=\text{erf}(\eta)\).

We’ll visualize all the solutions together in an interactive demo.

3.5.3 Case II: Known heat flux \(q_0''\)

We won’t go through all the math again, but will talk boundary conditions and the solution. Instead of the known surface temperature we had in Case I, we’ll use a known heat flux:

\[ q_0'' = -k\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\bigg|_0 \]

Note

The fact that \(\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \neq 0\) at \(x=0\) means we have heat transfer at the surface.

The solution is:

\[T(x,t) = T_i + \frac{2q_0''\sqrt{\alpha t/\pi}}{k}\exp\!\left(\frac{-x^2}{4\alpha t}\right) - \frac{q_0'' x}{k}\!\left(1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)\right)\]

Warning

Unphysical prediction at \(x = 0\):

\[ T(0,t) = T_i + \frac{2q_0''\sqrt{\alpha t/\pi}}{k} \]

grows without bound!

3.5.4 Case III: Convection \(h\) at the surface

Instead of a known temperature or known heat flux, our last boundary condition will be a known convective heat transfer coefficient:

\[ -k\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\bigg|_0 = h\bigl(T_\infty - T(0)\bigr). \]

The solution is \[ T(x,t) = T_i + (T_\infty - T_i)\!\left(1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)\right)- (T_\infty - T_i)\exp\!\left(\frac{hx}{k} + \frac{h^2\alpha t}{k^2}\right)\!\left[1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}} + \frac{h\sqrt{\alpha t}}{k}\right)\right] \].

3.5.5 Case IV: Oscillatory Temperature at the Surface

Our last boundary condition is

\[ T(0,t) = T_i + \Delta T \sin\omega t \]

for known oscillation amplitude \(\Delta T\) and known frequency \(\omega\). The solution:

\[ T(x,t) = T_0 + \Delta T\exp\!\left(-x\sqrt{\frac{\omega}{2\alpha}}\right)\cdot\sin\!\left(\omega t - x\sqrt{\frac{\omega}{2\alpha}}\right) \]

for \(T_0 \equiv \frac{T_\text{max}+T_\text{min}}{2}\) and \(\Delta T \equiv \frac{T_\text{max}-T_\text{min}}{2}\).

Tip

Can we predict the depth that the sinusoid influences via dimensional analysis? We would expect that the depth is going to be a function of the parameters in the problem: the oscillation frequency \(\omega\), the diffusivity \(\alpha\), and the amplitude \(\Delta T\). That is, we can write

\[ \text{depth} \sim \omega^a \cdot \alpha^b \cdot \Delta T^c \]

where powers \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) will be identified in order to make the equation dimensionally consistent. Depth has dimensions of length; frequency has dimensions of 1/time; diffusivity has dimensions of (length)^2/time, and temperature amplitude has dimensions of… temperature. We thus recognize that \(c=0\) – depth does not involve temperature; dividing \(\alpha\) by \(\omega\) gives a quantity with dimensions of (length)^2; and so

\[ \text{depth} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\omega}} \]

is a dimensionally consistent equation. The meaning: there will be a “wave” of heat that propagates into the soil when the surface is warm, but it is followed by a “wave” of coldness. These cancel each other out before either makes it deep into the soil, so there is a depth where the soil temperature is basically a constant – it doesn’t even know the surface is changing temperature. The equation tells us that depth will increase with thermal diffusivity, which makes sense: the thermal diffusivity is basically how readily temperature changes propagate through a material. That depth will decrease with increasing frequency: basically the hot and cold waves are produced closer together and cancel out earlier.

3.7.1 Material properties are sensitive to temperature

Let’s look back at the 1D conduction equation for a moment

\[ \rho c \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}( k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}) +\dot{q}. \]

Using the product rule to expand the spatial-variation term

\[ \rho c \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial k}{\partial x}\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}+k\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} +\dot{q} \]

we see that, if we are being rigorous, there is a \(\partial k/\partial x\) term. This is a nightmare! Thermal conductivity is a function of temperature: beryllium, for example, has a thermal conductivity of 161 W/m·K at 400 K and a value of 126 W/m·K at 600 K… a decrease of about 20%. We could write \(k\) as a function of \(T\) somehow, e.g. by fitting data to a polynomial, but we’ve still got to solve a nonlinear PDE!

To avoid issues like this, we typically assume material properties are constants, evaluated at ONE reasonable average temperature: if the hot boundary in a conduction problem is at 600 K, and the cold boundary is at 200 K, we look up the value of thermal conductivity at 400 K. That is THE thermal conductivity of the problem.

In cases where we don’t know the average, we can: make a guess for the average temperature, compute the solution, compute the average of that solution, and check to see if our guess was reasonably close. It probably will be fine – a 5% difference in thermal conductivity is not the biggest source of error in our analyses.

3.7.2 Where to get material properties?

A partial list of options:

  • Heat transfer texbooks usually have appendices with tables of values.
  • Websites like Wolfram Alpha have access to databases of material property values.
  • There are programming tools like CoolProp and SolidProp, which are particularly interesting if your analyses are being done in programming.
  • Some companies produce data sheets, and researchers sometimes publish data sheets. NIST has a lot of information.
Warning

It is good practice to cite the source of your data, but even if you do, it is still your responsibility if the value is wrong! This is particularly relevant in the age of AI: LLMs will give you property values, equations, etc., but there is always a chance the AI has misinterpreted a data set or hallucinated results. If something breaks because you got a bad material property from an LLM, you can blame the LLM if you want, but your company is going to blame you!

Verify values with multiple sources, or at least do a sanity check: e.g., metals have thermal conductivities of about 10 W/m·K to about 400 W/m·K (for the best conductors). If you are using a thermal conductivity of 0.012 W/m·K or 12000 W/m·K, alarm bells should be going off in your head!

# Transient

This week we are going to focus on transient conduction. In all cases, a solid object will be losing/gaining energy due to convective heat transfer. We are going to be thinking about temperature as a function of position AND time. That means our governing equations are PDEs! ... But actually we'll arrive at a few practical equations that will be useful in solving problems. 

This section is big on thinking about the assumptions in the problems. Our first model, the Lumped Capacitance Model, will allow us to assume that there is no spatial-variation in the temperature within the object (which dramatically simplifies the analysis). The next step up in complication, the One-Term Approximation, will let us think about spatial variation with certain limitations. The full solution has infinitely many terms in it... we try to avoid using that one!

We'll also think about the choice of boundary conditions: is it reasonable to say that the surface has a known temperature, or a known heat flux, or a known convection coefficient? There are differences in the solutions between these cases!

## The Lumped Capacitance Model

::: {.callout-tip}
### Video

Start with [this video link](https://canvas.colorado.edu/courses/136150/pages/lumped-capacitance), which covers the Biot Number and Lumped Capacitance Model. Then read the following.
:::

In engineering (and elsewhere), we sometimes talk about "the limiting factor" or "the bottleneck". Some assorted examples:

- There may be consumer demand for a new product, plenty of financial backing from investors, but the product is not taking off -- its limiting factor is the manufacturing time.
- Combustion is a chemical reaction that requires fuel, oxygen, and heat. If you spill some gasoline on the ground, there is plenty of fuel and oxygen available -- the limiting factor to the chemical reaction is heat. (Be careful though, combustion creates its own energy once it gets going!)
- In space travel, a returning spacecraft might have the right trajectory, the right parachutes, and a well-designed landing zone, but the limiting factor is heat. The vehicle is moving so fast that aerodynamic compression heats the surrounding air to thousands of degrees; the mission succeeds or fails entirely based on whether the heat shield can manage that thermal load.

The present discussion: we may have a situation in which a hot object needs to be cooled by a fluid passing over it. That process involves conduction of heat through the object (i.e., getting energy from its interior to its exterior) and convection out of the object (i.e., getting energy from the surface into the fluid). The "lumped capacitance model" assumes that the limiting factor of this process is the convection step.

### The Biot Number

Another way to talk about this limiting factor: the thermal resistance for conduction is much less than that for convection. Framed in terms of the resistor model: $R_\text{conduction} \ll R_\text{convection}$. Plugging-in the equations for these resistors

$$
\frac{L}{kA} \ll \frac{1}{hA} \implies \frac{hL}{k} \ll 1.
$$ 

The quantity $hL/k$ is a dimensionless number known as "The Biot Number"

$$
\text{Bi}\equiv \frac{hL_c}{k}
$$

and in general it is expressed in terms of the convective heat transfer coefficient $h$, the thermal conductivity of the solid object $k$, and a "characteristic length" $L_c$ that depends on the problem's geometry. Whenever $\text{Bi} \ll 1$, convection will be our limiting factor. In practice we say when $\text{Bi} \leq 0.1$.

::: {.callout-caution}
We will define another dimensionless number when studying convection, the Nusselt Number, that has the same definition as the Biot Number. The difference is: the Nusselt Number uses the thermal conductivity of the fluid, the Biot Number uses the thermal conductivity of the solid.
:::

::: {.column-margin}
A generally applicable definition of the characteristic length is $L_c \equiv (\text{volume})/(\text{surface area})$, but often we just choose a convenient lengthscale in the problem. E.g., for a sphere, some folks will just use the sphere radius.
:::

An implication of this observation is that the temperature within an object will have time to equilibrate with itself: any initial gradients in temperature will lead to heat flow from hot to cold until the object is approximately at uniform temperature. We can then say the object has one temperature (rather than a temperature distribution), and can analyze how that one temperature changes with time. This is the "lumped capacitance model/assumption".

::: {.column-margin}
Why "lumped capacitance"? "Lumping" means to disregard differences (in this case variation in temperature within an object) and "capacitance" is (probably) related to the idea that we are discharging voltage (temperature) within the object, similar to what happens in a capacitor circuit. I've never really written out an explanation before...
:::


### Problem analysis

We are going to imagine a solid object -- say a sphere or cylinder -- with known physical properties and geometry; and flow over that object, with known convective heat transfer coefficient $h$ and temperature $T_\infty$. A very practical question: if the object starts at a temperature $T_i$, how long does it take for the center of the object to reach $T_\text{cool}$?

::: {.column-margin}
Need more motivation? You just made a guaranteed-delicious cookie. It comes out of the oven too hot to eat -- how long do you have to wait until it hits that perfect temperature where the chocolate is melty, but you won't burn yourself?
:::

With the lumped capacitance assumption, we can write an energy balance for the object, assuming that the energy of the object is changing only due to convection:

$$
\frac{dE}{dt}=mc\frac{dT}{dt} = -hA(T - T_\infty).
$$

We will get better physical insight when we go through a non-dimensionalization process. If you are interested in the mathematical details, they'll be in the collapsed menu below.

::: {.callout-note collapse="true"}
### Click here to see the details of the non-dimensionalization.

We'll use a definition of dimensionless $\theta$ that is slightly different from the one we used with the fins

$$
\theta \equiv \frac{T - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty}
$$

and we will speculate that there is a "characteristic time" in the problem, $t_c$, to-be-determined. Then we can write a dimensionless time

$$
\tau \equiv \frac{t}{t_c}.
$$

Use the chain rule:

$$
\frac{d}{dt} = \frac{d\tau}{dt}\frac{d}{d\tau} = \frac{1}{t_c}\frac{d}{d\tau}
$$

and a little bit of calculus:

$$
\frac{dT}{d\tau}=\frac{d}{d\tau}\left(T_\infty + (T_i-T_\infty)\theta\right) = (T_i-T_\infty)\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}.
$$

Plugging-in to our energy balance
$$
mc\frac{(T_i-T_\infty)}{t_c}\frac{dT}{d\tau} = -hA(T_\infty + ((T_i-T_\infty)\theta-T_\infty)
$$

and doing some rearranging, we land at

$$
\frac{1}{t_c}\frac{d\theta}{d\tau} = -\frac{hA}{mc} \theta.
$$

Now, we haven't defined $t_c$ yet. Notice that $\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}$ and $\theta$ are dimensionless... that means $mc/(hA)$ must have dimensions of time. For mathematical convenience and for physical insight, we can define $t_c = mc/(hA)$ as our characteristic time.
:::

We land on a differential equation, expressed in dimensionless form:

$$
\frac{d\theta}{d\tau}=-\theta
$$

where

$$
\theta \equiv \frac{T - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty}
$$

and

$$
\tau \equiv \frac{t}{t_c}\quad\text{with }t_c \equiv \frac{mc}{hA}.
$$

The differential equation is solved by recognizing the initial condition is $\theta(0)=1$:

$$ 
\theta(\tau) = \exp(-\tau).
$$ {#eq-nondimLC}

Or, converted back into a dimensional equation:

$$
T(t) = T_\infty + (T_i - T_\infty)\exp\left(-\frac{hA}{mc}t\right).
$$ {#eq-dimLC}

#### Some observations

1. This analysis reveals that, as long as $\text{Bi}\leq 0.1$, the cooling-off process is the same for all materials: given by @eq-nondimLC, an exponential decay. Dimensionally, @eq-dimLC, the decay is stretched/shrunk by $t_c$, and the initial and final temperature values depend on $T_i$ and $T_\infty$. But all the possible variations in the parameters collapse onto the single dimensionless solution, @eq-nondimLC.

2. Are the factors in the characteristic time intuitive? The definition is $t_c = mc/(hA)$, and it appears in the equation as $\exp(-hAt/(mc))$. A larger value of $h$ means better cooling, and so the temperature should decrease faster... indeed, $h$ appears upstairs in the exponential. A larger object, meaning larger mass $m$, will cool slower... it makes sense that $m$ is downstairs. What about the other factors?

3. The characteristic time is sometimes called "the time constant", and this language is used in many fields (e.g., you might recall it from your circuits class). In truth, it takes infinite time for the object to reach $T_\infty$: the decaying exponential asymptotes to $T_\infty$, but never gets there. However, it is not productive to say "it takes literally forever to cool down". Instead we reference the time constant. Develop this common language: after one time constant, the process is 63% of the way towards steady state. After two time constants, it is 86% of the way there. And we might say "its time constant is $10\times$ as high", which practically means it takes $10\times$ as long to cool.

### Series Solution

For a sphere, the conduction equation can be simplified to

$$
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}= \alpha \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2 \frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right).
$$

The assumptions are mostly standard, think through how we'd get here. One is worth a bit of discussion: we are going to assume that an average $h$ is valid for the entire sphere. Actually the "local" value of $h$ -- that is, the value of $h$ at a particular location on the surface -- is going to depend on the location on the surface. Think about boundary layers, flow separation, turbulent wakes, etc. ... we're not going to assume those things don't exist, but we are going to claim we can average all that behavior into a single value of $h$.

What are the initial/boundary conditions? Because the PDE is first-order in time, we need one initial condition; and because it is second order in $r$, we need two boundary conditions. They are:

- We know the initial temperature distribution is uniform: $T(r,0) = T_i$.
- We know the solution has to be physically realistic, so $T(r,t)$ is bounded. (Where does this come up? We might see that solving the PDE leads to a term in the general solution $\sim a_1/r$, which goes to infinity at $r=0$. To keep that from happening, we pick $a_1=0$.)
- We know that the heat arriving at the surface via conduction leaves the surface due to convection: $-k\frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\bigg\rvert_R = h(T(R)-T_\infty)$.

With these, the problem is solvable after a lot of math:
$$
\theta(r,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_n \exp\!\left(-\zeta_n^2 \cdot \text{Fo}\right) \frac{\sin\!\left(\zeta_n \cdot \frac{r}{R}\right)}{\zeta_n r/R}
$$ {#eq-seriessol}

where $\text{Fo} \equiv \alpha t/R^2$ is known as "The Fourier Number", which can be thought of as a dimensionless time.

::: {.column-margin}
The Fourier Number is basically a measure of the progression of heat through an object: if time is large, or the thermal diffusivity is large, or the object is small, the Fourier Number is big.
:::

The "eigenvalues" of the problem, $\zeta_n$, are the solutions to the equation

$$
1 - \zeta_n \cot \zeta_n = \text{Bi}.
$$

(If you are interested in the details you can read up on the "separation of variables" technique for solving PDEs -- $\zeta_n$ are special values that connect the time- and space-dependent aspects of the solution.) We also have coefficients

$$
C_n = \frac{4\left[\sin\zeta_n - \zeta_n\cos\zeta_n\right]}{2\zeta_n - \sin(2\zeta_n)}.
$$

So: if we have calculated our Biot Number, we can calculate our $\zeta_n$ values (there are infinitely many of them), and then our coefficient $C_n$ values (infinitely many). Then we can compute the infinite summation to determine the temperature at any radial location, at any time.

Calculating infinity terms? Hopefully there is a better way...

### The One-Term Approximation

The eigenvalues of the problem are calculated in ascending order: $\zeta_1 < \zeta_2 < \zeta_3 < ... $. For the sphere problem, the eigenvalues are about $\pi$ apart, and that is more-or-less the case for any geometry. What does that mean for the decaying exponential term in @eq-seriessol? The $n=2$ term is going to be much much smaller than the $n=1$ term (assuming $\text{Fo}$ is not too close to zero).

With this observation, we make the "one-term approximation": due to that decaying exponential, we neglect the $n=2$, $n=3$, ... , $n=1000$, ... terms from the summation: only the $n=1$ substantially contributes. Then 

$$
\theta = C_1 \exp\!\left(-\zeta_1^2 \cdot \text{Fo}\right) \frac{\sin\!\left(\zeta_1 \cdot \frac{r}{R}\right)}{\zeta_1 r/R}
$$

and, after unpacking, we understand our temperature distribution $T(r,t)$. We typically say this equation is valid and useful when $\text{Bi}>0.1$ and $\text{Fo}>0.2$.

A note on $\text{Fo}>0.2$: A typical problem we'd hope to solve is "find the time when...". Even in the case where we don't know what that time is *a priori* (ahead of time), we can make the assumption that the one-term approximation is valid, do the calculation, and then check that indeed $\text{Fo}>0.2$. In any practical problem, we are not interested in an abrupt event: the Fourier Number is going to be plenty large for the one-term approximation.

The depth spans from $x=0$ to $\infty$, and the idea is: we are adding heat at the surface (by four different methods), how does the temperature in the initially cold soil change with time? We will solve for $T(x,t)$.

The starting point for all heat-addition methods is the conduction equation with no heat generation:

$$
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=\alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}.
$$

A PDE that is first-order in time and second-order in position, and so we'll need one initial condition and two boundary conditions. For all the cases described below, we'll use

$$
T(x,0)=T_i
$$

... the initial condition that all of the soil is at a uniform temperature $T_i$ at $t=0$. We'll also use

$$
T(\infty,t)=T_i
$$

... the boundary condition that, at a depth of infinity, the temperature is at the initial temperature $T_i$. The second boundary condition will vary problem-to-problem.


### Case I: Known surface temperature $T_s$

We will assume that the surface temperature is at a known value such that our second boundary condition is

$$
\text{BC1:} \quad T(0,t) = T_s.
$$

I'll go through the derivation in this collapsable note, if you are interested.

::: {.callout-note collapse="true"}
#### Click here to see the details of the derivation.

The solution idea: the heat addition at the surface is going to be felt at a greater depth $x$ as time $t$ increases, and so we are going to try to couple those variables together. (If you are interested in looking up another PDE solution technique, this one is called the similarity solution method.)

There is a way to systematically determine the way that the variables should be coupled, but I'm just going to skip that and say: let's try this dimensionless variable

$$
\eta = \frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}.
$$

If this all works out, we will arrive at a solution $T(\eta)$, and we can plug-in the definition of $\eta$ to get $T(x,t)$.

Let's again use a $\theta$ (a non-dimensional temperature), defined as 

$$
\theta \equiv \frac{T - T_s}{T_i - T_s}
$$

Then

$$
\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2}
$$

becomes:

$$
\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 \theta}{\partial x^2}
$$ {#eq-nondimHeat}

Some chain rule calculations:

$$
\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial^2\theta}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\frac{\partial\eta}{\partial x}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2} = \frac{1}{4\alpha t}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2}
$$

$$
\frac{\partial\theta}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial t}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}}\cdot\frac{1}{t}\cdot\frac{d\theta}{d\eta}
$$

Substituting into @eq-nondimHeat yields

$$
\frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}\,t}\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \alpha\cdot\frac{1}{4\alpha t}\frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2}.
$$

With some algebra, we can show that $\frac{-x}{4\sqrt{\alpha t}\,t}$ equals $-\frac{1}{2}\eta$, and so we have

$$
-2\eta\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = \frac{d^2\theta}{d\eta^2}
$$

...an ODE! Secretly, pretty much all of the PDE solution techniques involve turning the equation into an ODE (or a few ODEs) because we are comfortable solving ODEs. Importantly for this technique: we have arrived at a dimensionless equation that does not explicitly depend on $x$ or $t$, it instead is a function of the $\eta$ we defined. This means our assumption of the existence of a coupled variable $\eta$ was a good one.

We turned the equation into an ODE for $\theta(\eta)$, so we must make our initial and boundary conditions apply to $\theta(\eta)$. Examining our definitions for $\theta$ and $\eta$, we recognize that the initial condition becomes

$$
\quad T(x,0) = T_i \implies \theta(\infty) = 1,
$$

the first boundary condition becomes 

$$
T(\infty,t) = T_i \implies \theta(\infty) = 1
$$

(same as the initial condition, an important check to make sure this method works), and the second boundary condition becomes

$$
T(0,t) = T_s \implies \theta(0) = 0.
$$

We'll solve by integration... it might be clearer to define $M(\eta)\equiv d\theta/d\eta$. Then

$$
\frac{dM}{d\eta} = -2\eta M \quad \Rightarrow \quad \int\frac{dM}{M} = \int -2\eta\,d\eta
$$

which means

$$
\ln M = -\eta^2 + c_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad M = d_1\exp(-\eta^2)
$$

that is:

$$
\frac{d\theta}{d\eta} = d_1\exp(-\eta^2).
$$

Then we simply need to do another integration... the trouble is, the right-hand-side of this equation has no nice antiderivative. We can, and do, write

$$
\theta(\eta) = d_1\int_0^{\eta}\exp(-z^2)\,dz + d_2
$$

where $z$ is a dummy variable. Applying the boundary/initial conditions:

$$
\theta(0) = 0 = d_1\int_0^0\cdots\,dz + d_2 \;\Rightarrow\; d_2 = 0
$$

and 

$$
\theta(\infty) = 1 = d_1\int_0^{\infty}\exp(-z^2)\,dz = d_1 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}.
$$

:::

We arrive at our solution:

$$
\theta(\eta) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^{\eta}\exp(-z^2)\,dz.
$$

This function is common enough that it has a name: "The Error Function". We sometimes denote it $\theta(\eta)=\text{erf}(\eta)$.

We'll visualize all the solutions together in [an interactive demo](demos/semi_infinite_demo.qmd).



### Case II: Known heat flux $q_0''$

We won't go through all the math again, but will talk boundary conditions and the solution. Instead of the known surface temperature we had in Case I, we'll use a known heat flux:

$$
q_0'' = -k\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\bigg|_0
$$

::: {.callout-note}
The fact that $\frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \neq 0$ at $x=0$ means we have heat transfer at the surface.
:::

The solution is:


$$T(x,t) = T_i + \frac{2q_0''\sqrt{\alpha t/\pi}}{k}\exp\!\left(\frac{-x^2}{4\alpha t}\right) - \frac{q_0'' x}{k}\!\left(1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)\right)$$

::: {.callout-warning }
Unphysical prediction at $x = 0$:

$$
T(0,t) = T_i + \frac{2q_0''\sqrt{\alpha t/\pi}}{k}
$$

grows without bound!
:::


### Case III: Convection $h$ at the surface

Instead of a known temperature or known heat flux, our last boundary condition will be a known convective heat transfer coefficient:

$$
-k\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}\bigg|_0 = h\bigl(T_\infty - T(0)\bigr).
$$

The solution is
$$
T(x,t) = T_i + (T_\infty - T_i)\!\left(1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}}\right)\right)- (T_\infty - T_i)\exp\!\left(\frac{hx}{k} + \frac{h^2\alpha t}{k^2}\right)\!\left[1 - \text{erf}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{\alpha t}} + \frac{h\sqrt{\alpha t}}{k}\right)\right]
$$.


### Case IV: Oscillatory Temperature at the Surface

Our last boundary condition is

$$
T(0,t) = T_i + \Delta T \sin\omega t
$$

for known oscillation amplitude $\Delta T$ and known frequency $\omega$. The solution:


$$
T(x,t) = T_0 + \Delta T\exp\!\left(-x\sqrt{\frac{\omega}{2\alpha}}\right)\cdot\sin\!\left(\omega t - x\sqrt{\frac{\omega}{2\alpha}}\right)
$$

for $T_0 \equiv \frac{T_\text{max}+T_\text{min}}{2}$ and $\Delta T \equiv \frac{T_\text{max}-T_\text{min}}{2}$.

::: {.callout-tip}
Can we predict the depth that the sinusoid influences via dimensional analysis? We would expect that the depth is going to be a function of the parameters in the problem: the oscillation frequency $\omega$, the diffusivity $\alpha$, and the amplitude $\Delta T$. That is, we can write 

$$
\text{depth} \sim \omega^a \cdot \alpha^b \cdot \Delta T^c
$$

where powers $a$, $b$, and $c$ will be identified in order to make the equation dimensionally consistent. Depth has dimensions of length; frequency has dimensions of 1/time; diffusivity has dimensions of (length)^2/time, and temperature amplitude has dimensions of... temperature. We thus recognize that $c=0$ -- depth does not involve temperature; dividing $\alpha$ by $\omega$ gives a quantity with dimensions of (length)^2; and so

$$
\text{depth} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\omega}}
$$

is a dimensionally consistent equation. The meaning: there will be a "wave" of heat that propagates into the soil when the surface is warm, but it is followed by a "wave" of coldness. These cancel each other out before either makes it deep into the soil, so there is a depth where the soil temperature is basically a constant -- it doesn't even know the surface is changing temperature. The equation tells us that depth will increase with thermal diffusivity, which makes sense: the thermal diffusivity is basically how readily temperature changes propagate through a material. That depth will decrease with increasing frequency: basically the hot and cold waves are produced closer together and cancel out earlier.


:::

### Material properties are sensitive to temperature

Let's look back at the 1D conduction equation for a moment

$$
\rho c \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}( k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x}) +\dot{q}.
$$

Using the product rule to expand the spatial-variation term

$$
\rho c \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial k}{\partial x}\frac{\partial T}{\partial x}+k\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial x^2} +\dot{q}
$$

we see that, if we are being rigorous, there is a $\partial k/\partial x$ term. This is a nightmare! Thermal conductivity is a function of temperature: beryllium, for example, has a thermal conductivity of 161 W/m·K at 400 K and a value of 126 W/m·K at 600 K... a decrease of about 20%. We could write $k$ as a function of $T$ somehow, e.g. by fitting data to a polynomial, but we've still got to solve a nonlinear PDE!

To avoid issues like this, we typically assume material properties are constants, evaluated at ONE reasonable average temperature: if the hot boundary in a conduction problem is at 600 K, and the cold boundary is at 200 K, we look up the value of thermal conductivity at 400 K. That is THE thermal conductivity of the problem.

In cases where we don't know the average, we can: make a guess for the average temperature, compute the solution, compute the average of that solution, and check to see if our guess was reasonably close. It probably will be fine -- a 5% difference in thermal conductivity is not the biggest source of error in our analyses.

### Where to get material properties?

A partial list of options:

- Heat transfer texbooks usually have appendices with tables of values.
- Websites like [Wolfram Alpha](https://www.wolframalpha.com/input?i=thermal+conductivity+of+copper+at+447+K) have access to databases of material property values.
- There are programming tools like [CoolProp](https://coolprop.org) and [SolidProp](https://github.com/jaktk/SolidProps), which are particularly interesting if your analyses are being done in programming.
- Some companies produce data sheets, and researchers sometimes publish data sheets. NIST has a lot of information.

::: {.callout-warning}
It is good practice to cite the source of your data, but even if you do, it is still your responsibility if the value is wrong! This is particularly relevant in the age of AI: LLMs will give you property values, equations, etc., but there is always a chance the AI has misinterpreted a data set or hallucinated results. If something breaks because you got a bad material property from an LLM, you can blame the LLM if you want, but your company is going to blame you!

Verify values with multiple sources, or at least do a sanity check: e.g., metals have thermal conductivities of about 10 W/m·K to about 400 W/m·K (for the best conductors). If you are using a thermal conductivity of 0.012 W/m·K or 12000 W/m·K, alarm bells should be going off in your head!
:::
