This week we are going to focus on fins, a useful item in our engineering design toolkit. The idea is this: Hot surface! We need to cool it! But the fluid passing over it isn’t creating a high enough \(\dot{Q}\). If our electronics are producing 200 W, but we are only able to remove 100 W, we are going to cook our electronics.

We could increase the flow over the object (bigger fan), change the medium of the flow (water-cooled is better than air-cooled), or put the object in a room with lower temperature (data centers are usually very cold). We are going to focus on another approach: increasing surface area. We will do so by putting “fins” on the object – objects with a high surface area to volume ratio, e.g. numerous 10 mm-long, 2 mm-diameter cylinders. These will wick heat from the object via conduction, and their large surface area will allow greater opportunity to deposit that heat in the fluid via convection.

We’ll examine another version of conservation of energy – this time, 1D only, though we’ll include some convection out (radially) within that description. “The Fin Equation” is an ordinary differential equation for temperature as a function of position, and it will include in it considerations of: the thermal conductivity, the convection coefficient, and the geometry of the fin. In the case of constant cross-section fins, we can solve the Fin Equation analytically.

2.1 Think First

Recall

  1. What is the thermal conductivity \(k\)? What is the convection coefficient \(h\)?
  2. What is the thermal resistance analogy? \(\dot{Q}=\Delta T/R\), but what is \(R\)?
  3. Why does increasing surface area increase convective heat transfer?

Look Ahead

  1. How do we decide the size and number of fins?
  2. How do material choices (copper vs. steel, air vs. water) affect fin performance?
  3. What do we mean by a fin being “effective” and “efficient”? Do they mean different things?

2.2 The Fin Equation

2.2.1 Problem setup and solution

Here is a general geometry for a single fin:

Here, a slender cylindrical fin is attached to a hot surface at \(x=0\). The fin has a known geometry: known cross-section \(A_c(x)\) (defined at a position \(x\), and we are allowing for the possibility that \(A_c\) depends on \(x\)) and known length \(L\). At every location \(x\), we know the perimeter around the fin, \(P(x)\), and we’ll assume we know the fin material (and thus \(k\)) and have looked up a value of the convection coefficient (so \(h\) is known). We are going to write an energy balance at an arbitrary position \(x\), assuming:

  1. We are at steady state
  2. The fin is slender such that the temperature is uniform within each cross-section
  3. Constant/averaged value of \(h\)

The first two points allow us to think about \(T(x)\) instead of worrying about \(T(x,y,z,t)\).

NoteClick here to see the details of the derivation.

We write a conservation of energy for the \(\Delta x\) segment, making use of the assumption of steadiness to say

\[ \frac{dE}{dt} = 0 = q''(x) \cdot A_c(x) - q''(x+\Delta x) \cdot A_c(x+\Delta x) - h \cdot P(x) \cdot \Delta x \cdot (T(x) - T_\infty). \]

The terms in that equation represent: conductive heat transfer in from the left cross-section, conduction out through the right cross-section, and convection out through the outer surface. Here we describe the surface area as the perimeter multiplied by \(\Delta x\). Think about a cylinder: the area of the curved surface is \(\pi D\), the perimeter of a circle, multiplied by the axial length.

Using Fourier’s Law \(q'' = -k\frac{dT}{dx}\):

\[ 0 = \left(-k A_c\frac{dT}{dx}\right)\bigg|_x - \left(-k A_c\frac{dT}{dx}\right)\bigg|_{x+\Delta x} - h \cdot P(x) \cdot \Delta x \cdot (T(x) - T_\infty). \]

Then we divide by \(\Delta x\) and take the limit as \(\Delta x \to 0\) (definition of derivative), leaving…

The Fin Equation: \[ 0 = \frac{d}{dx}\left(k A_c(x) \frac{dT}{dx}\right) - h P(x) (T(x) - T_\infty) \]

a second-order ordinary differential equation that, when solved with two boundary conditions, will give us the temperature distribution in the fin \(T(x)\). From this we will be able to calculate the total heat transfer out of the fin.

2.3 The solution for uniform cross-section

2.3.1 Assumptions

We are going to analyze the case where the cross-section and perimeter are constants, not functions of \(x\). We’ll also assume that \(k\) is a constant. Then the Fin Equation becomes:

\[ 0 = k A_c\frac{d^2T}{dx^2} - hP(T(x) - T_\infty) \]

and we can combine the parameters in the problem into one constant

\[ 0 = \frac{d^2T}{dx^2} - \frac{hP}{k A_c}(T(x) - T_\infty). \]

It’s looking easier to deal with already… we’re going to do one extra setup step.

2.3.2 Non-Dimensionalization

It is nice to non-dimensionalize equations. Framed within the context of the fin problem: we know that \(k\), \(h\), \(A_c\) and \(P\) are relevant, and they indeed appear in that combined parameter we just arrived at. But how does \(L\) factor in? Can we say without solving the equation completely? Indeed, after non-dimensionalizing the equation, we will have greater insight. The non-dimensionalized equation is going to be easier to solve too!

First we are going to define a non-dimensional temperature:

\[ \theta(x) \equiv \frac{T(x) - T_\infty}{T_b - T_\infty}. \]

This is convenient because, when \(T=T_b\), the non-dimensional \(\theta =1\), and when \(T=T_\infty\), the non-dimensional \(\theta =0\). We should intuitively expect our solution to start at \(\theta=1\) and asymptote towards \(\theta=0\) as we move away from the fin base.

We are also going to define a non-dimensional position:

\[ X\equiv \frac{x}{L}. \]

Again, it’s convenient: \(X\) goes from \(0\) to \(1\) from fin base to tip. We then will reframe the differential equation as \(\theta(X)\) instead of \(T(x)\). Using the chain rule:

\[ \frac{dT}{dx}=\frac{dX}{dx}\frac{dT}{dX} = \frac{1}{L}\frac{dT}{dX} \]

and

\[ \frac{d^2T}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{dT}{dx}\right) = \frac{dX}{dx}\frac{d}{dX}\left(\frac{1}{L}\frac{dT}{dX}\right)=\frac{1}{L^2}\frac{d^2T}{dX^2}. \]

Then plugging-in the definition of \(\theta\):

\[ \frac{d^2T}{dX^2} = \frac{d^2}{dX^2}\left((T_b-T_\infty)\theta + T_\infty\right) = (T_b-T_\infty)\frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2}. \]

If we include these results in the constant cross-section differential equation we’ll have

\[ 0= \frac{(T_b-T_\infty)}{L^2}\frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - \frac{hP}{k A_c}(T(x) - T_\infty) \]

and, with some algebra, we can settle on a non-dimensional form of the differential equation:

\[ 0= \frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - m^2\theta \]

with

\[ m=\sqrt{\frac{hPL^2}{kA_c}} \]

a dimensionless constant.

As we hinted above, one boundary condition that we will use is \(\theta(0)=1\) (from \(T(0)=T_b\)). The second is a bit more controversial… we’ll get into that in the next section. But let’s think about what we have arrived at with this non-dimensionalization:

  1. We have identified a dimensionless parameter \(m\) that is the single parameter in the problem. Naively we might think that fully understanding fins would require us to monitor the effect of \(h\) while keeping all the other parameters constant, then monitor the effect of \(A_c\) while keeping all the other parameters constant, then monitor the effect of \(L\), … . It turns out that we can just think about varying one parameter \(m\), and we wisely recognize that, for example, doubling the fin length will have the same effect as increasing the convection coefficient by four. We capture the push-and-pull of all the dimensional parameters in a single dimensionless parameter.
  2. An equation of the form \(y'' - c y =0\) (what we have in the non-dimensional equation) is a bit easier to solve than an equation of the form \(y'' - cy = d\) (which we had in the dimensional equation).

2.3.3 Boundary Conditions

The problem is not well-posed until we figure out the boundary conditions. The first boundary condition is not controversial:

\[ T(x=0) = T_b \implies \theta(X=0) = 1. \]

The second is not as obvious – we have to make an assumption.

2.3.3.1 Known temperature

We could assume that

\[ T(L) = T_\infty \implies \theta(1) = 0. \]

However, this presumes knowledge of the temperature at the tip. Do we know for certain that it gets all the way to \(\theta = 0\) by \(X = 1\)? That will not be the case with a short fin, but it might be reasonable for a long fin.

2.3.3.2 Adiabatic tip

We could assume that the tip is “adiabatic”:

\[ \frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_L = 0 \implies \frac{d\theta}{dX}\bigg\rvert_1 = 0. \]

Rationale: most of the energy is convected out along the length, so the amount convected out at \(x = L\) is approximately zero. That is, the tip is adiabatic.

TipThink

What would the plot of the solution look like at \(X=1\) if this boundary condition applied? Specifically think about the slope of the curve.

2.3.3.3 Tip heat transfer \(\sim h\)

We could assume that the tip has convection with coefficient \(h\). Then the heat that is conducted to the tip will be convected out of the tip: \[ -k\frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_L = h(T(L) - T_\infty) \implies -\frac{k}{L}\frac{d\theta}{dX}\bigg\rvert_1 = h\theta(1). \]

This is perhaps the most defendable boundary condition, but “is the juice worth the squeeze?” (One of my favorite sayings.) This boundary condition is the most difficult to implement.

Given the many assumptions that have gone into this analysis, the error from using other boundary conditions is probably not a big deal! It is most common to assume an adiabatic tip.

2.3.4 Analytical Solution

Let’s solve

\[ 0= \frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - m^2\theta \]

with

\[ m=\sqrt{\frac{hPL^2}{kA_c}} \]

subject to boundary conditions

\[ \begin{align*} &\theta(0)=1\\ &\theta(1)=0. \end{align*} \]

(The solution for the adiabatic tip will be given in the demo below.) We may be tempted to say the general solution is

\[ \theta(X) = c_1 \exp{(-mX)} + c_2 \exp{(mX)} \]

which is totally correct, but the algebra that follows when applying the boundary conditions is going to be annoying. Instead, let’s remember the definitions of

\[ \sinh(z) \equiv (\exp(z)-\exp(-z))/2 \text{~and~}\cosh(z)\equiv (\exp(z)+\exp(-z))/2 \]

and write the solution as

\[ \theta(X) = d_1 \sinh(mX) + d_2 \cosh(mX). \]

Applying the boundary condition at \(X=0\):

\[ \theta(0) = 1 = d_1 \sinh(0) + d_2 \cosh(0) \implies d_2 = 1. \]

Applying the boundary condition at \(X=1\):

\[ 0 = d_1 \sinh(m) + \cosh(m) \implies d_1 = -\frac{\cosh(m)}{\sinh(m)} = -\frac{1}{\tanh(m)}. \]

Therefore, our solution is

\[ \theta(X) = \cosh(mX) - \frac{\sinh(mX)}{\tanh(m)} \]

2.3.5 Converting back to \(T(x)\)

We can use the definitions of \(\theta\) and \(X\) to convert the equation back to dimensional variables:

\[ T(x)=T_\infty + (T_b - T_\infty)\left[\cosh\!\left(\frac{mx}{L}\right)-\frac{\sinh\!\left(\frac{mx}{L}\right)}{\tanh(m)}\right]. \]

2.4 Calculating the fin heat transfer

With the temperature profile \(T(x)\) known, we have at least two ways of calculating the fin heat transfer rate \(\dot{Q}_f\).

2.4.1 Using \(h\)

More intuitively: we might look to the basic equation for convective heat transfer \(\dot{Q} = h A_\text{surf}(T-T_\infty)\) and consider how to account for a temperature that changes with position. Sounds like a job for… integration over the area:

\[ \dot{Q}_f = \int h (T(x)-T_\infty) dA_\text{surface}. \]

For a cylinder (as depicted above) we would write the area as \(A_\text{surface} = \pi D x\) and so \(dA_\text{surface} = \pi D dx\).

2.4.2 Using \(k\)

We also might wisely recognize that, since we have assumed steady state, the rate that heat is conducted into the fin must match the rate that heat is convected out of the fin. That is:

\[ \dot{Q}_f = -k \frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_0 A_c(0). \]

2.5 Fin Performance Metrics

We can characterize the fin performance using two metrics.

2.5.1 Effectiveness

The fin effectiveness, \(\epsilon_f\), tells us how much better the fin is doing at removing heat than the plain surface. That is:

\[ \epsilon_f = \frac{\text{the amount of heat transfer w/ fin}}{\text{the amount without the fin}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_f}{h \cdot A_c(0)(T_b - T_\infty)} \]

where the downstairs includes \(A_c(0)\), the area of the fin base. It should be the case that \(\epsilon>1\), else the fin does a worse job than the plain surface! Typically \(\epsilon>2\).

2.5.2 Efficiency

The fin efficiency, \(\eta_f\), gives us a measure of our fin’s performance against a perfect fin where the entirety of the fin surface is at \(T_b\):

\[ \eta_f \equiv \frac{\text{the amount of heat transfer w/ fin}}{\text{the amount if the whole fin was at }T_b} = \frac{\dot{Q}_f}{h \cdot A_\text{surf} \cdot (T_b - T_\infty)}. \]

We will have \(0 < \eta_f < 1\). When \(\eta_f\) is small, it means that the temperature profile in the fin has quickly dropped from \(T_b\) to close to \(T_\infty\), i.e. as we see in the \(m=10\) case above. In such a case, the fin might be nearly as effective with half the length, meaning we may save in material costs. Moreover, a shorter fin will likely be more durable, and it may be the case that a shorter fin allows for better fluid circulation, thus increasing \(h\).

2.8.1 Inclusion in the resistor model

It is common to include numerous fins on an object – if you are going to attach one, might as well attach a few! From a practical perspective, we are interested in calculating the total amount of heat transfer from the object, and that includes the heat transfer from the \(N\) fins and from the “unfinned” area of the object (the area between the fins).

We are assuming that \(N\) fins will create \(N\dot{Q}_f\) heat transfer. But adding more fins is going to hinder the flow – the cool ambient air will not flow as freely over the fins, which will decrease the value of \(h\).

We can model this situation as a collection of resistors in parallel: \(N\) resistors for the \(N\) fins, and one more for the unfinned area.

Reminder: the fin resistors are \(R_f = 1/(\eta_f h A_f)\), and we’ll use a convection resistor for the base \(R_b = 1/(h A_b)\). Then, according to the rule for combining resistors in parallel, the total resistance of this circuit is

\[ \frac{1}{R_\text{tot}}=\frac{1}{R_f}+\frac{1}{R_f}+\frac{1}{R_f}+\dots+\frac{1}{R_b}. \]

That is,

\[ R_\text{tot}= \frac{1}{N\eta_f h A_f + h A_b}. \]

With this we can calculate the total heat transfer from the finned object

\[ \dot{Q} = \frac{T_b-T_\infty}{R_\text{tot}}. \]

Here, a slender cylindrical fin is attached to a hot surface at $x=0$. The fin has a known geometry: known cross-section $A_c(x)$ (defined at a position $x$, and we are allowing for the possibility that $A_c$ depends on $x$) and known length $L$. At every location $x$, we know the perimeter around the fin, $P(x)$, and we'll assume we know the fin material (and thus $k$) and have looked up a value of the convection coefficient (so $h$ is known). We are going to write an energy balance at an arbitrary position $x$, assuming:

1. We are at steady state
2. The fin is slender such that the temperature is uniform within each cross-section
3. Constant/averaged value of $h$

The first two points allow us to think about $T(x)$ instead of worrying about $T(x,y,z,t)$.

::: {.callout-note collapse="true"}
### Click here to see the details of the derivation.

We write a conservation of energy for the $\Delta x$ segment, making use of the assumption of steadiness to say

$$
\frac{dE}{dt} = 0 = q''(x) \cdot A_c(x) - q''(x+\Delta x) \cdot A_c(x+\Delta x) - h \cdot P(x) \cdot \Delta x \cdot (T(x) - T_\infty).
$$

The terms in that equation represent: conductive heat transfer in from the left cross-section, conduction out through the right cross-section, and convection out through the outer surface. Here we describe the surface area as the perimeter multiplied by $\Delta x$. Think about a cylinder: the area of the curved surface is $\pi D$, the perimeter of a circle, multiplied by the axial length.

Using Fourier's Law $q'' = -k\frac{dT}{dx}$:

$$
0 = \left(-k A_c\frac{dT}{dx}\right)\bigg|_x  - \left(-k A_c\frac{dT}{dx}\right)\bigg|_{x+\Delta x} - h \cdot P(x) \cdot \Delta x \cdot (T(x) - T_\infty).
$$

Then we divide by $\Delta x$ and take the limit as $\Delta x \to 0$ (definition of derivative), leaving...
:::

The Fin Equation:
$$
0 = \frac{d}{dx}\left(k A_c(x) \frac{dT}{dx}\right) - h P(x) (T(x) - T_\infty)
$$

a second-order ordinary differential equation that, when solved with two boundary conditions, will give us the temperature distribution in the fin $T(x)$. From this we will be able to calculate the total heat transfer out of the fin.



## The solution for uniform cross-section

::: {.callout-tip}
### Video

Start with [this video link](https://canvas.colorado.edu/courses/136150/pages/fin-uniform-ac), which covers the solution process. Then read the following.
:::

### Assumptions

We are going to analyze the case where the cross-section and perimeter are constants, not functions of $x$. We'll also assume that $k$ is a constant. Then the Fin Equation becomes:

$$
0 = k A_c\frac{d^2T}{dx^2} - hP(T(x) - T_\infty)
$$

and we can combine the parameters in the problem into one constant

$$
0 = \frac{d^2T}{dx^2} - \frac{hP}{k A_c}(T(x) - T_\infty).
$$

It's looking easier to deal with already... we're going to do one extra setup step.

### Non-Dimensionalization

It is nice to non-dimensionalize equations. Framed within the context of the fin problem: we know that $k$, $h$, $A_c$ and $P$ are relevant, and they indeed appear in that combined parameter we just arrived at. But how does $L$ factor in? Can we say without solving the equation completely? Indeed, after non-dimensionalizing the equation, we will have greater insight. The non-dimensionalized equation is going to be easier to solve too!

First we are going to define a non-dimensional temperature:

$$
\theta(x) \equiv \frac{T(x) - T_\infty}{T_b - T_\infty}.
$$

This is convenient because, when $T=T_b$, the non-dimensional $\theta =1$, and when $T=T_\infty$, the non-dimensional $\theta =0$. We should intuitively expect our solution to start at $\theta=1$ and asymptote towards $\theta=0$ as we move away from the fin base.

We are also going to define a non-dimensional position:

$$
X\equiv \frac{x}{L}.
$$

Again, it's convenient: $X$ goes from $0$ to $1$ from fin base to tip. We then will reframe the differential equation as $\theta(X)$ instead of $T(x)$. Using the chain rule:

$$
\frac{dT}{dx}=\frac{dX}{dx}\frac{dT}{dX} = \frac{1}{L}\frac{dT}{dX}
$$

and

$$
\frac{d^2T}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{dT}{dx}\right) = \frac{dX}{dx}\frac{d}{dX}\left(\frac{1}{L}\frac{dT}{dX}\right)=\frac{1}{L^2}\frac{d^2T}{dX^2}.
$$

Then plugging-in the definition of $\theta$:

$$
\frac{d^2T}{dX^2} = \frac{d^2}{dX^2}\left((T_b-T_\infty)\theta + T_\infty\right) = (T_b-T_\infty)\frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2}.
$$

If we include these results in the constant cross-section differential equation we'll have

$$
0= \frac{(T_b-T_\infty)}{L^2}\frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - \frac{hP}{k A_c}(T(x) - T_\infty)
$$

and, with some algebra, we can settle on a non-dimensional form of the differential equation:

$$
0= \frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - m^2\theta
$$

with

$$
m=\sqrt{\frac{hPL^2}{kA_c}}
$$

a dimensionless constant.

As we hinted above, one boundary condition that we will use is $\theta(0)=1$ (from $T(0)=T_b$). The second is a bit more controversial... we'll get into that in the next section. But let's think about what we have arrived at with this non-dimensionalization:

1. We have identified a dimensionless parameter $m$ that is the single parameter in the problem. Naively we might think that fully understanding fins would require us to monitor the effect of $h$ while keeping all the other parameters constant, then monitor the effect of $A_c$ while keeping all the other parameters constant, then monitor the effect of $L$, ... . It turns out that we can just think about varying one parameter $m$, and we wisely recognize that, for example, doubling the fin length will have the same effect as increasing the convection coefficient by four. We capture the push-and-pull of all the dimensional parameters in a single dimensionless parameter.
2. An equation of the form $y'' - c y =0$ (what we have in the non-dimensional equation) is a bit easier to solve than an equation of the form $y'' - cy = d$ (which we had in the dimensional equation).

### Boundary Conditions

The problem is not well-posed until we figure out the boundary conditions. The first boundary condition is not controversial:

$$
T(x=0) = T_b \implies \theta(X=0) = 1.
$$

The second is not as obvious -- we have to make an assumption.

#### Known temperature

We could assume that

$$
T(L) = T_\infty \implies \theta(1) = 0.
$$

However, this presumes knowledge of the temperature at the tip. Do we know for certain that it gets all the way to $\theta = 0$ by $X = 1$? That will not be the case with a short fin, but it might be reasonable for a long fin.

#### Adiabatic tip

We could assume that the tip is "adiabatic":

$$
\frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_L = 0 \implies \frac{d\theta}{dX}\bigg\rvert_1 = 0.
$$

Rationale: most of the energy is convected out along the length, so the amount convected out at $x = L$ is approximately zero. That is, the tip is adiabatic.

::: {.callout-tip}
##### Think
What would the plot of the solution look like at $X=1$ if this boundary condition applied? Specifically think about the slope of the curve.
:::

#### Tip heat transfer $\sim h$

We could assume that the tip has convection with coefficient $h$. Then the heat that is conducted to the tip will be convected out of the tip:
$$
-k\frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_L = h(T(L) - T_\infty) \implies -\frac{k}{L}\frac{d\theta}{dX}\bigg\rvert_1 = h\theta(1).
$$

This is perhaps the most defendable boundary condition, but "is the juice worth the squeeze?" (One of my favorite sayings.) This boundary condition is the most difficult to implement.

Given the many assumptions that have gone into this analysis, the error from using other boundary conditions is probably not a big deal! It is most common to assume an adiabatic tip.


### Analytical Solution

Let's solve

$$
0= \frac{d^2\theta}{dX^2} - m^2\theta
$$

with 

$$
m=\sqrt{\frac{hPL^2}{kA_c}}
$$

subject to boundary conditions

$$
\begin{align*}
&\theta(0)=1\\
&\theta(1)=0.
\end{align*}
$$

(The solution for the adiabatic tip will be given in the demo below.) We may be tempted to say the general solution is

$$
\theta(X) = c_1 \exp{(-mX)} + c_2 \exp{(mX)}
$$

which is totally correct, but the algebra that follows when applying the boundary conditions is going to be annoying. Instead, let's remember the definitions of 

$$
\sinh(z) \equiv (\exp(z)-\exp(-z))/2 \text{~and~}\cosh(z)\equiv (\exp(z)+\exp(-z))/2
$$

and write the solution as

$$
\theta(X) = d_1 \sinh(mX) + d_2 \cosh(mX).
$$

Applying the boundary condition at $X=0$:

$$
\theta(0) = 1 = d_1 \sinh(0) + d_2 \cosh(0) \implies d_2 = 1.
$$

Applying the boundary condition at $X=1$:

$$
0 = d_1 \sinh(m) + \cosh(m) \implies d_1 = -\frac{\cosh(m)}{\sinh(m)} = -\frac{1}{\tanh(m)}.
$$

Therefore, our solution is

$$
\theta(X) = \cosh(mX) - \frac{\sinh(mX)}{\tanh(m)}
$$

### Converting back to $T(x)$

We can use the definitions of $\theta$ and $X$ to convert the equation back to dimensional variables:

$$
T(x)=T_\infty + (T_b - T_\infty)\left[\cosh\!\left(\frac{mx}{L}\right)-\frac{\sinh\!\left(\frac{mx}{L}\right)}{\tanh(m)}\right].
$$

## Calculating the fin heat transfer

With the temperature profile $T(x)$ known, we have at least two ways of calculating the fin heat transfer rate $\dot{Q}_f$.

### Using $h$

More intuitively: we might look to the basic equation for convective heat transfer $\dot{Q} = h A_\text{surf}(T-T_\infty)$ and consider how to account for a temperature that changes with position. Sounds like a job for... integration over the area:


$$
\dot{Q}_f = \int h (T(x)-T_\infty) dA_\text{surface}.
$$

For a cylinder (as depicted above) we would write the area as $A_\text{surface} = \pi D x$ and so $dA_\text{surface} = \pi D dx$.


### Using $k$

We also might wisely recognize that, since we have assumed steady state, the rate that heat is conducted into the fin must match the rate that heat is convected out of the fin. That is:

$$
\dot{Q}_f = -k \frac{dT}{dx}\bigg\rvert_0 A_c(0).
$$

## Fin Performance Metrics

We can characterize the fin performance using two metrics.

### Effectiveness

The fin effectiveness, $\epsilon_f$, tells us how much better the fin is doing at removing heat than the plain surface. That is:

$$
\epsilon_f = \frac{\text{the amount of heat transfer w/ fin}}{\text{the amount without the fin}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_f}{h \cdot A_c(0)(T_b - T_\infty)}
$$

where the downstairs includes $A_c(0)$, the area of the fin base. It should be the case that $\epsilon>1$, else the fin does a worse job than the plain surface! Typically $\epsilon>2$.

### Efficiency

The fin efficiency, $\eta_f$, gives us a measure of our fin's performance against a perfect fin where the entirety of the fin surface is at $T_b$:

$$
\eta_f \equiv \frac{\text{the amount of heat transfer w/ fin}}{\text{the amount if the whole fin was at }T_b} = \frac{\dot{Q}_f}{h \cdot A_\text{surf} \cdot (T_b - T_\infty)}.
$$

We will have $0 < \eta_f < 1$. When $\eta_f$ is small, it means that the temperature profile in the fin has quickly dropped from $T_b$ to close to $T_\infty$, i.e. as we see in the $m=10$ case above. In such a case, the fin might be nearly as effective with half the length, meaning we may save in material costs. Moreover, a shorter fin will likely be more durable, and it may be the case that a shorter fin allows for better fluid circulation, thus increasing $h$.

### Inclusion in the resistor model
It is common to include numerous fins on an object -- if you are going to attach one, might as well attach a few! From a practical perspective, we are interested in calculating the total amount of heat transfer from the object, and that includes the heat transfer from the $N$ fins and from the "unfinned" area of the object (the area between the fins).

::: {.column-margin}
We are assuming that $N$ fins will create $N\dot{Q}_f$ heat transfer. But adding more fins is going to hinder the flow -- the cool ambient air will not flow as freely over the fins, which will decrease the value of $h$.
:::

We can model this situation as a collection of resistors in parallel: $N$ resistors for the $N$ fins, and one more for the unfinned area.

Reminder: the fin resistors are $R_f = 1/(\eta_f h A_f)$, and we'll use a convection resistor for the base $R_b = 1/(h A_b)$. Then, according to the rule for combining resistors in parallel, the total resistance of this circuit is

$$
\frac{1}{R_\text{tot}}=\frac{1}{R_f}+\frac{1}{R_f}+\frac{1}{R_f}+\dots+\frac{1}{R_b}.
$$

That is,

$$
R_\text{tot}= \frac{1}{N\eta_f h A_f + h A_b}.
$$

With this we can calculate the total heat transfer from the finned object

$$
\dot{Q} = \frac{T_b-T_\infty}{R_\text{tot}}.
$$
