4  External Convection

We’re going to dig deeper into convection, and this week will be “external convection” – fluid passing over an object.

4.2 What is \(h\)?

TipVideo

Start with this linked video, an introduction to convection.

4.2.1 Circumventing the boundary layer details

We’ve been using \(h\) in the context of

\[ \dot{Q} = h A_\text{surface}(T_\text{surface}-T_\infty). \tag{4.1}\]

Notice:

  1. It is separate from the \(\Delta T=T_\text{surface}-T_\infty\).
  2. It is separate from the surface area \(A_\text{surface}\).
  3. It must include material properties and flow speed in it somehow… including flow phenomena such as boundary layers, flow separation, and turbulence.

The third point should make you nervous – flow phenomena are complicated! Can you tell me the velocity profile for turbulent flow around a sphere? Somehow that is factored-in to \(h\)!

There is good news: \(h\) is a quantity we invented to summarize the effects of flow and material properties. Even as we dig deeper into convection, we maintain the definition given in Equation 4.1, including the use of \(\Delta T = T_\text{surface}-T_\infty\). It is much simpler to just think about the surface temperature and ambient temperature than it is to deal with the detailed temperature distribution spanning those values! This is the power of \(h\)… it allows us to do that.

4.2.2 Local and average \(h\)

We’ve been a bit sloppy in our discussion of the convective heat transfer coefficient, saying for example that \(h=22\) W/m²·K for flow over an object. More rigorously, and in view of the dependence on the flow profile: \(h\) can be described as a “local value”, as in the value at a particular position on the surface; or as an “averaged value”, as in effective/averaged value over an entire surface. Technically we have been talking about an averaged value \(\overline{h}\), which would be defined as

\[ \overline{h}=\frac{1}{A_\text{surface}}\int h(\mathbf{x})dS \]

an average of the local values evaluated over the entire surface.

4.2.3 An experimentalist’s approach

Rather than solve the coupled nonlinear partial differential equations necessary to determine the heat flux at each point on the surface, we might instead think about how to determine the relationship between the heat transfer out of the surface and the problem parameters. And to make it a bit more specific: if we are interested in convection, we are thinking about \(h\).

We should expect that \(h\) depends on:

  • The incoming flow velocity, \(U_\infty\). This sets how much cool fluid is passing over the object, and also is related to boundary layers, flow separation, and turbulence.
  • Also relevant to boundary layers, etc., is the fluid viscosity, \(\mu\).
  • The specific heat, \(c_p\), which is how much energy it takes to heat up a kilogram of fluid by one Kelvin. If it takes more energy to change the temperature of the fluid, it is going to do a better job at removing heat from the object.
  • Tied to that is the density: how much mass is passing by?
  • The thermal conductivity of the fluid, \(k_f\), is also relevant. Convection is ostensibly moving energy downstream, but it depends on the rate at which we can conduct energy perpendicular to the flow, out of the object.
  • Position or length is relevant, too. The distance from the leading edge, \(L\), has an effect on the flow profile.

That is, we should have \(h(U_\infty,\mu,c_p,\rho, k_f, L)\).

Note

Recall the Buckingham-\(\Pi\) Theorem: A problem with \(n\) parameters/variables, described by \(r\) fundamental dimensions, is governed by at most \(n-r\) dimensionless parameters.

So: 7 parameters, and 4 dimensions are necessary to quantify them: mass, length, time, and temperature. Thus we should expect 3 dimensionless groups. They are:

\[ \text{The Reynolds Number }\text{Re}_L\equiv \frac{\rho U_\infty L}{\mu} \]

\[ \text{The Nusselt Number }\text{Nu}_L\equiv \frac{h L}{k_f} \]

\[ \text{The Prandtl Number }\text{Pr}\equiv \frac{\nu}{\alpha} = \frac{\mu/\rho}{k_f/(\rho c_p)} = \frac{\mu c_p}{k_f}. \]

A few notes:

  • The Reynolds and Nusselt Numbers often include a subscript to denote what characteristic length is being used in the definition, e.g. for a cylinder with diameter \(D\), we would use $D = UD/.
  • The Nusselt Number looks exactly like the Biot Number: \(hL/k\). The difference is that the Nusselt Number uses the thermal conductivity of the fluid, the Biot Number uses the thermal conductivity of the solid.
  • We might seek an averaged \(\overline{h}\), in which case we’d use an averaged \(\overline{\text{Nu}}\).
  • The Prandtl Number is a material property that can be looked up just like the thermal conductivity.

4.2.4 Algorithm for Solving Convection Problems

  1. Retrieve all relevant fluid properties. It is common to use “the film temperature” as the reference temperature: $T_ (T_+ T_)/2. (Sometimes the correlations are defined based on another reference temperature – pay attention!)
  2. Identify your model geometry and look up the relevant Nusselt Number “correlation” – e.g., below I have the averaged correlation for an infinite cylinder of diameter \(D\). It’s a doozy.
  3. Calculate the relevant Reynolds Number and use it, along with the Prandtl Number, in the Nusselt Number correlation.
  4. Unpack the Nusselt Number correlation: \(\overline{Nu}_L \equiv \overline{h}L/k\) means \(\overline{h} = (k/L) \overline{Nu}_L\).
  5. Use \(\overline{h}\) in the convective cooling law: $ = A_(T_-T_).

4.3 The Flat Plate

The geometry is: a uniform flow with speed \(U_\infty\), temperature \(T_\infty\), and known material properties approaches a flat plate that is either held at a known temperature \(T_s\), or that produces a known heat flux \(q_s''\). Due to friction at the plate, a boundary layer structure will form, with an associated velocity distribution that spans from zero (no-slip condition) to \(U_\infty\) far from the plate. We will similarly have a “thermal” boundary layer, where the temperature distribution spans from a surface value (\(T_s\) if the surface is constant temperature, but varying if it’s a heat flux boundary condition) to \(T_\infty\) far from the plate. How are these distributions connected?

Start with this linked video on the flat-plate geometry.

4.3.1 Boundary layer thickness

You may recall from fluid mechanics that we can use dimensional analysis to determine the dependence of the boundary layer thickness, \(\delta\), on the problem parameters. A reminder: we have a no-slip condition at the plate’s surface that sets up a competition between “advection”, the flow going downstream characterized by \(U_\infty\); and viscous diffusion, the friction between neighboring parcels traveling at different speeds characterized by \(\nu\equiv \mu/\rho\). The boundary layer grows as we move further from the leading edge, distance given by \(L\), and so we should expect \(\delta(L,U_\infty,\nu)\). Check the dimensions of this equation:

\[ \delta \sim \sqrt{\frac{\nu L}{U_\infty}} \]

and the physics makes sense: the boundary layer will grow more rapidly if it more diffusive, but will be smaller at a given position if the flow speed is high. This can be rearranged to:

\[ \frac{\delta}{L} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\nu }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\text{Re}_L}}. \]

We have a similar competition for the thermal boundary layer, thickness \(\delta_T\): now it’s a thermal diffusivity, \(\alpha\), competing with advection. The diffusivities have the same dimensions, (length)²/(time), and so we expect the same dimensional relationship between the parameters:

\[ \delta_T \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha L}{U_\infty}} \]

or

\[ \frac{\delta_T}{L} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\nu}\frac{\nu }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\text{Pr}\cdot\text{Re}_L}}. \]

Observe:

\[ \frac{\delta}{\delta_T} = \sqrt{\text{Pr}}. \]

So the Prandtl Number tells us the ratio between the momentum and thermal boundary layer thicknesses.

4.3.2 Numerical solutions

After non-dimensionalizing the equations (in a way that includes \(\delta\) and \(\delta_T\)), we can arrive at: the conservation of \(x\)-momentum:

\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{\text{Re}}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} \]

and the conservation of energy

\[ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t}+u \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{\text{Re}\cdot\text{Pr}}\frac{\partial^2\theta}{\partial y^2} \]

with \(u(0)=0\), \(u(\infty)=1\) and \(\theta(0)=0\), \(\theta(\infty)=1\). These equations are not solvable analytically, but we can solve them numerically. (If you took MCEN 3030 with me, you solved the first one in a different form, after plugging-in conservation of mass.) So we can determine a numerical value for the velocity gradient at the surface, which is related to the viscous drag; and can determine a numerical value for the temperature gradient at the surface, which is related to the heat flux into the fluid. Let’s see how these are connected.

Thinking back to fluid mechanics and Newton’s Law of Viscosity, we can relate the shear stress at the surface \(\tau_\text{surface}\) to the viscosity \(\mu\) and velocity gradient at the surface: \[ \tau_\text{surface} = \mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\bigg|_0 \approx \mu\frac{U_\infty - 0}{\delta}. \]

Here, we also gave an order-of-magnitude estimate of the value, based on the fact that: over the boundary layer thickness \(\delta\), we will see a change in velocity of \(U_\infty-0\).

We can do something similar with convection, using Newton’s Law of Cooling:

\[ h(T_\text{surface}-T_\infty) = -k_f\frac{\partial T}{\partial y}\bigg|_0 \approx k_f\frac{T_\infty - T_\text{surf}}{\delta_T}. \]

Using \(\delta = L\sqrt{1/\text{Re}_L}\) and \(\delta_T = L\sqrt{1/(\text{Pr}\cdot\text{Re}_L)}\), we can write

\[ \tau_\text{surface} = \mu\frac{U_\infty}{L}\sqrt{\text{Re}} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \frac{h}{k_f} = \frac{\sqrt{\text{Re}\cdot\text{Pr}}}{L}. \]

From the friction coefficient \(C_f \equiv \frac{\tau_\text{surf}}{\frac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2}\) (non-dimensional, used to determine drag on wall):

\[C_f = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}}\]

\[\text{Nu}_L \equiv \frac{hL}{k_f} = \sqrt{\text{Re}_L \cdot \text{Pr}}\]

Therefore \(\text{Nu} = \frac{C_f}{2}\text{Re}\cdot\sqrt{\text{Pr}}\), where \(C_f\) is well-documented from numerical calculations.

4.5.1 Flow over blunt objects

For flow over blunt objects, flow separation and wakes complicate the description of heat transfer. It is very interesting to think about the local \(h\) or \(\text{Nu}\), as a function of position, but practically we use averaged values of \(\overline{h}\) and \(\overline{\text{Nu}}\) in our calculations.

Tip

There are often several options for the correlations. The choice is often: use a complicated expression, but it applies to a wider range of Reynolds Number; or use a simpler expression, but it applies to a narrower range. If you are certain your design falls within a narrow \(\text{Re}\) range, it is likely fine to stick with the simpler expression.

From Incropera and DeWitt, a simple correlation is:

\[ \overline{\text{Nu}}_D \equiv \frac{\overline{h}D}{k} = C\,\text{Re}_D^m\,\text{Pr}^{1/3} \qquad \text{for Pr}\geq 0.7 \]

where the values of \(C\) and \(m\) depend on the Reynolds Number:

\(\text{Re}_D\) \(C\) \(m\)
0.4–4 0.989 0.330
4–40 0.911 0.385
40–4000 0.683 0.466
4000–40000 0.193 0.618
40000–400000 0.027 0.805

(All properties evaluated at \(T_\text{film} = \frac{T_s + T_\infty}{2}\).) This is a very generic description and has been applied to “cylinders” of different cross-sectional shapes, e.g. 

This description is generic enough that it has also been applied to cylinders of different shapes, e.g. a diamond-shaped cylinder (\(5\times10^3\)–\(10^5\): \(C = 0.246\), \(m = 0.588\)).

A slight improvement in accuracy with this modification:

\[\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = C\cdot\text{Re}_D^m\cdot\text{Pr}^n\cdot\left(\frac{\text{Pr}}{\text{Pr}_s}\right)^{1/4} \qquad \text{(Pr}_s\text{ evaluated at }T_s\text{)}\]

with \(C\) and \(m\) from above, and

\[n = \begin{cases}0.36 & \text{if Pr}>10 \\ 0.37 & \text{if Pr}\leq10\end{cases}\]

Or, more complicated but valid for all \(\text{Re}_D\) (cylinder):

\[\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 0.3 + \frac{0.62\,\text{Re}_D^{1/2}\,\text{Pr}^{1/3}}{\left[1+(0.4/\text{Pr})^{2/3}\right]^{1/4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{\text{Re}_D}{282000}\right)^{5/8}\right]^{4/5}\]

In the limit \(\text{Re}_D \to 0\) (“creeping flow”):

\[\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 0.3 = \frac{\overline{h}D}{k_\text{fluid}}\]

\(\Rightarrow\) For small objects: \(D\downarrow \;\Rightarrow\; \overline{h}\uparrow\)

4.6 External Flow — The Sphere

The same physics applies to the sphere as well. The correlation is:

\[ \overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 2 + \left(0.4\,\text{Re}_D^{1/2} + 0.06\,\text{Re}_D^{2/3}\right)\text{Pr}^{0.4}\cdot\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_s}\right)^{1/4} \tag{4.2}\]

All properties evaluated at \(T_\infty\) except \(\mu_s\) (at surface).

4.6.1 Worked Example — Copper Sphere Cooling

A copper sphere with diameter 10 mm begins at 75°C and is to be cooled to 35°C by air. The air has speed 10 m/s and temperature 23°C.

We look up the properties of copper at 55°C: \(k = 399\) W/m·K, \(c = 388\) J/kg·K, \(\rho = 8933\) kg/m³.

For the sphere correlation, Equation 4.2, most of the fluid properties are evaluated at \(T_\infty\): \(\mu = 182.6\times 10^{-7}\) Pa·s, \(\rho = 1.17\) kg/m³, \(\text{Pr} = 0.708\), and \(k = 0.0251\) W/m·K. We also need the fluid viscosity evaluated at the surface temperature, and we use an average 55°C: \(\mu_s = 197.8\times10^{-7}\) Pa·s.

Start with calculating the Reynolds Number:

\[ \text{Re}_D = \frac{\rho U_\infty D}{\mu} = 6440 \]

Then use the correlation from above to get

\[ \overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 47.1 = \frac{\overline{h}D}{k_f} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \overline{h} = 118\;\text{W/m}^2\text{K} \]

Step 3: Check Bi:

\[\text{Bi} = \frac{\overline{h}\cdot D/2}{k_\text{copper}} = 0.0015 < 0.1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{LC model is good}\]

Step 4: Apply lumped capacitance:

\[\theta = \frac{T - T_\infty}{T_i - T_\infty} = \exp\!\left(\frac{-t}{t_c}\right), \qquad t_c \equiv \frac{mc}{\overline{h}A_\text{surf}}\]

At \(T = T_f = 35°C\): \(\theta = \frac{35-23}{75-23}\), solve for \(t\).

4.7.2 Nusselt Number Correlation for Tube Banks

How about a more practical geometry? One of the most important applications of heat transfer is the heat exchanger (covered in detail later). And one of the most common designs is the “shell-and-tube” heat exchanger. With this device, a collection of pipes are enclosed in a shell. If we want to cool the contents of those pipes, we pass a cold fluid through the shell (and vice-versa). We talk about the “number of passes”: the cold fluid will pass around part of every pipe, say the last 20% of the length, and then be redirected around a barrier such that the cold fluid can pass over the pipes again, and then around another barrier for another pass, and so one. We’ll model this situation as flow passing over a collection of cylinders, and (at least for now) we’ll make the assumption that the pipes are at a constant temperature.

It is very common to see a shell-and-tube heat exchanger as a condenser in the Rankine Cycle, responsible for turning steam into a liquid. Remember that, during a phase change, water maintains a constant temperature – it is not at all unreasonable to assume that \(T_\text{s}\) (the pipe surface temperature) is a constant in this application.

Our hope is to find a \(\overline{\text{Nu}}_D\) that describes the an entire array.

A correlation for \(N_L \geq 20\), \(1000 \leq \text{Re}_{D,\max} \leq 2\times10^6\), \(0.7 \leq \text{Pr} \leq 500\)):

\[ \overline{\text{Nu}}_D = C_1\,\text{Re}_{D,\max}^m\cdot\text{Pr}^{0.36}\cdot\left(\frac{\text{Pr}}{\text{Pr}_s}\right)^{1/4} \]

Properties evaluated at \(T_\text{avg} = \frac{T_\text{in}+T_\text{out}}{2}\) except for \(\text{Pr}_s\), evaluated at the surface temperature.

Re based on the highest flow rate \(V_\text{max}\):

Aligned: \(V_\text{max} = \frac{S_T}{S_T - D}V_\infty\)

Staggered: If \(S_D = \left[S_L^2 + \left(\frac{S_T}{2}\right)^2\right]^{1/2} < \frac{S_T+D}{2}\), then \(V_\text{max} = \frac{S_T}{2(S_D-D)}V_\infty\). Else, same as aligned case.

4.8.1 The Log-Mean Temperature Difference

When it comes to predicting total heat transfer, it is not the case that:

\[\dot{Q} \neq N\overline{h}\cdot\pi DL\cdot(T_\text{surf}-T_\infty) \quad \text{(crossed out — this overestimates } \dot{Q}\text{)}\]

Because \(\dot{Q}\) can’t simply scale with \(N\) and \(T_\text{surf}-T_\infty\): the fluid warms up as it passes through the bank.

Instead, use the log-mean temperature difference:

\[\boxed{\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{(T_s - T_i) - (T_s - T_o)}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_s - T_i}{T_s - T_o}\right)}} \quad = \text{"log-mean temperature difference"}\]

a better representation of the average local \(\Delta T\), where \(T_i\) = inlet temperature, \(T_o\) = outlet temperature of external flow.

Find \(T_o\) from:

\[\frac{T_s - T_o}{T_s - T_i} \approx \exp\!\left(\frac{-\pi D N \overline{h}}{\rho V N_T S_T c_p}\right)\]

(Here \(N_T\) = number of tubes per row, \(V\) = free-stream velocity.)

Then:

\[\boxed{\dot{Q} = N\overline{h}\,\pi DL\cdot\Delta T_{lm}} \qquad \text{(Bank of }N\text{ tubes)}\]

Recall the Buckingham-$\Pi$ Theorem: A problem with $n$ parameters/variables, described by $r$ fundamental dimensions, is governed by at most $n-r$ dimensionless parameters.
:::

So: 7 parameters, and 4 dimensions are necessary to quantify them: mass, length, time, and temperature. Thus we should expect 3 dimensionless groups. They are:

$$
\text{The Reynolds Number }\text{Re}_L\equiv \frac{\rho U_\infty L}{\mu}
$$

$$
\text{The Nusselt Number }\text{Nu}_L\equiv \frac{h L}{k_f}
$$

$$
\text{The Prandtl Number }\text{Pr}\equiv \frac{\nu}{\alpha} = \frac{\mu/\rho}{k_f/(\rho c_p)} = \frac{\mu c_p}{k_f}.
$$

A few notes:

- The Reynolds and Nusselt Numbers often include a subscript to denote what characteristic length is being used in the definition, e.g. for a cylinder with diameter $D$, we would use $\text{Re}_D = \rho U_\infty D/\mu.
- The Nusselt Number looks exactly like the Biot Number: $hL/k$. The difference is that the Nusselt Number uses the thermal conductivity of the fluid, the Biot Number uses the thermal conductivity of the solid.
- We might seek an averaged $\overline{h}$, in which case we'd use an averaged $\overline{\text{Nu}}$.
- The Prandtl Number is a material property that can be looked up just like the thermal conductivity.


### Algorithm for Solving Convection Problems

1. Retrieve all relevant fluid properties. It is common to use "the film temperature" as the reference temperature: $T_\text{film} \equiv (T_\infty + T_\text{surface})/2. (Sometimes the correlations are defined based on another reference temperature -- pay attention!)
2. Identify your model geometry and look up the relevant Nusselt Number "correlation" -- e.g., below I have the averaged correlation for an infinite cylinder of diameter $D$. It's a doozy.
3. Calculate the relevant Reynolds Number and use it, along with the Prandtl Number, in the Nusselt Number correlation.
4. Unpack the Nusselt Number correlation: $\overline{Nu}_L \equiv \overline{h}L/k$ means $\overline{h} = (k/L) \overline{Nu}_L$.
5. Use $\overline{h}$ in the convective cooling law: $\dot{Q} = \overline{h}A_\text{surface}(T_\text{surface}-T_\infty).


## The Flat Plate

The geometry is: a uniform flow with speed $U_\infty$, temperature $T_\infty$, and known material properties approaches a flat plate that is either held at a known temperature $T_s$, or that produces a known heat flux $q_s''$. Due to friction at the plate, a boundary layer structure will form, with an associated velocity distribution that spans from zero (no-slip condition) to $U_\infty$ far from the plate. We will similarly have a "thermal" boundary layer, where the temperature distribution spans from a surface value ($T_s$ if the surface is constant temperature, but varying if it's a heat flux boundary condition) to $T_\infty$ far from the plate. How are these distributions connected?

### Boundary layer thickness

You may recall from fluid mechanics that we can use dimensional analysis to determine the dependence of the boundary layer thickness, $\delta$, on the problem parameters. A reminder: we have a no-slip condition at the plate's surface that sets up a competition between "advection", the flow going downstream characterized by $U_\infty$; and viscous diffusion, the friction between neighboring parcels traveling at different speeds characterized by $\nu\equiv \mu/\rho$. The boundary layer grows as we move further from the leading edge, distance given by $L$, and so we should expect $\delta(L,U_\infty,\nu)$. Check the dimensions of this equation:

$$
\delta \sim \sqrt{\frac{\nu L}{U_\infty}}
$$

and the physics makes sense: the boundary layer will grow more rapidly if it more diffusive, but will be smaller at a given position if the flow speed is high. This can be rearranged to:

$$
\frac{\delta}{L} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\nu }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\text{Re}_L}}.
$$

We have a similar competition for the thermal boundary layer, thickness $\delta_T$: now it's a thermal diffusivity, $\alpha$, competing with advection. The diffusivities have the same dimensions, (length)²/(time), and so we expect the same dimensional relationship between the parameters:

$$
\delta_T \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha L}{U_\infty}}
$$

or 

$$
\frac{\delta_T}{L} \sim \sqrt{\frac{\alpha }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{\alpha}{\nu}\frac{\nu }{U_\infty L}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\text{Pr}\cdot\text{Re}_L}}.
$$

Observe:

$$
\frac{\delta}{\delta_T} = \sqrt{\text{Pr}}.
$$

So the Prandtl Number tells us the ratio between the momentum and thermal boundary layer thicknesses.

### Numerical solutions

After non-dimensionalizing the equations (in a way that includes $\delta$ and $\delta_T$), we can arrive at: the conservation of $x$-momentum:

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+u \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{\text{Re}}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}
$$

and the conservation of energy

$$
\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t}+u \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}+v\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = \frac{1}{\text{Re}\cdot\text{Pr}}\frac{\partial^2\theta}{\partial y^2}
$$

with $u(0)=0$, $u(\infty)=1$ and $\theta(0)=0$, $\theta(\infty)=1$. These equations are not solvable analytically, but we can solve them numerically. (If you took MCEN 3030 with me, you solved the first one in a different form, after plugging-in conservation of mass.) So we can determine a numerical value for the velocity gradient at the surface, which is related to the viscous drag; and can determine a numerical value for the temperature gradient at the surface, which is related to the heat flux into the fluid. Let's see how these are connected.

Thinking back to fluid mechanics and Newton's Law of Viscosity, we can relate the shear stress at the surface $\tau_\text{surface}$ to the viscosity $\mu$ and velocity gradient at the surface:
$$
\tau_\text{surface} = \mu\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\bigg|_0 \approx \mu\frac{U_\infty - 0}{\delta}.
$$

Here, we also gave an order-of-magnitude estimate of the value, based on the fact that: over the boundary layer thickness $\delta$, we will see a change in velocity of $U_\infty-0$.

We can do something similar with convection, using Newton's Law of Cooling:

$$
h(T_\text{surface}-T_\infty) = -k_f\frac{\partial T}{\partial y}\bigg|_0 \approx k_f\frac{T_\infty - T_\text{surf}}{\delta_T}.
$$

Using $\delta = L\sqrt{1/\text{Re}_L}$ and $\delta_T = L\sqrt{1/(\text{Pr}\cdot\text{Re}_L)}$, we can write

$$
\tau_\text{surface} = \mu\frac{U_\infty}{L}\sqrt{\text{Re}} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \frac{h}{k_f} = \frac{\sqrt{\text{Re}\cdot\text{Pr}}}{L}.
$$

From the friction coefficient $C_f \equiv \frac{\tau_\text{surf}}{\frac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2}$ (non-dimensional, used to determine drag on wall):

$$C_f = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}}$$

$$\text{Nu}_L \equiv \frac{hL}{k_f} = \sqrt{\text{Re}_L \cdot \text{Pr}}$$

Therefore $\text{Nu} = \frac{C_f}{2}\text{Re}\cdot\sqrt{\text{Pr}}$, where $C_f$ is well-documented from numerical calculations.

### Flow over blunt objects

For flow over blunt objects, flow separation and wakes complicate the description of heat transfer. It is very interesting to think about the local $h$ or $\text{Nu}$, as a function of position, but practically we use averaged values of $\overline{h}$ and $\overline{\text{Nu}}$ in our calculations.

::: {.callout-tip}
There are often several options for the correlations. The choice is often: use a complicated expression, but it applies to a wider range of Reynolds Number; or use a simpler expression, but it applies to a narrower range. If you are certain your design falls within a narrow $\text{Re}$ range, it is likely fine to stick with the simpler expression.
:::


From Incropera and DeWitt, a simple correlation is:

$$
\overline{\text{Nu}}_D \equiv \frac{\overline{h}D}{k} = C\,\text{Re}_D^m\,\text{Pr}^{1/3} \qquad \text{for Pr}\geq 0.7
$$

where the values of $C$ and $m$ depend on the Reynolds Number:

| $\text{Re}_D$ | $C$ | $m$ |
|---|---|---|
| 0.4–4 | 0.989 | 0.330 |
| 4–40 | 0.911 | 0.385 |
| 40–4000 | 0.683 | 0.466 |
| 4000–40000 | 0.193 | 0.618 |
| 40000–400000 | 0.027 | 0.805 |

(All properties evaluated at $T_\text{film} = \frac{T_s + T_\infty}{2}$.) This is a very generic description and has been applied to "cylinders" of different cross-sectional shapes, e.g. 

This description is generic enough that it has also been applied to cylinders of different shapes, e.g. a diamond-shaped cylinder ($5\times10^3$–$10^5$: $C = 0.246$, $m = 0.588$).

A slight improvement in accuracy with this modification:

$$\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = C\cdot\text{Re}_D^m\cdot\text{Pr}^n\cdot\left(\frac{\text{Pr}}{\text{Pr}_s}\right)^{1/4} \qquad \text{(Pr}_s\text{ evaluated at }T_s\text{)}$$

with $C$ and $m$ from above, and

$$n = \begin{cases}0.36 & \text{if Pr}>10 \\ 0.37 & \text{if Pr}\leq10\end{cases}$$

Or, more complicated but valid for all $\text{Re}_D$ (cylinder):

$$\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 0.3 + \frac{0.62\,\text{Re}_D^{1/2}\,\text{Pr}^{1/3}}{\left[1+(0.4/\text{Pr})^{2/3}\right]^{1/4}}\left[1+\left(\frac{\text{Re}_D}{282000}\right)^{5/8}\right]^{4/5}$$

In the limit $\text{Re}_D \to 0$ ("creeping flow"):

$$\overline{\text{Nu}}_D = 0.3 = \frac{\overline{h}D}{k_\text{fluid}}$$

$\Rightarrow$ For small objects: $D\downarrow \;\Rightarrow\; \overline{h}\uparrow$

The same physics applies to the sphere as well. The correlation is:

All properties evaluated at $T_\infty$ except $\mu_s$ (at surface).

A copper sphere with diameter 10 mm begins at 75°C and is to be cooled to 35°C by air. The air has speed 10 m/s and temperature 23°C.

We look up the properties of copper at 55°C: $k = 399$ W/m·K, $c = 388$ J/kg·K, $\rho = 8933$ kg/m³.

For the sphere correlation, @eq-sphere-corr, most of the fluid properties are evaluated at $T_\infty$: $\mu = 182.6\times 10^{-7}$ Pa·s, $\rho = 1.17$ kg/m³, $\text{Pr} = 0.708$, and $k = 0.0251$ W/m·K. **Step 3:** Check Bi:

### Nusselt Number Correlation for Tube Banks

How about a more practical geometry? One of the most important applications of heat transfer is the heat exchanger (covered in detail later). And one of the most common designs is the "shell-and-tube" heat exchanger. With this device, a collection of pipes are enclosed in a shell. If we want to cool the contents of those pipes, we pass a cold fluid through the shell (and vice-versa). We talk about the "number of passes": the cold fluid will pass around part of every pipe, say the last 20% of the length, and then be redirected around a barrier such that the cold fluid can pass over the pipes again, and then around another barrier for another pass, and so one. We'll model this situation as flow passing over a collection of cylinders, and (at least for now) we'll make the assumption that the pipes are at a constant temperature.

::: {.column-margin}
It is very common to see a shell-and-tube heat exchanger as a condenser in the Rankine Cycle, responsible for turning steam into a liquid. Remember that, during a phase change, water maintains a constant temperature -- it is not at all unreasonable to assume that $T_\text{s}$ (the pipe surface temperature) is a constant in this application.
:::

### The Log-Mean Temperature Difference

When it comes to predicting total heat transfer, it is **not** the case that:

$$\dot{Q} \neq N\overline{h}\cdot\pi DL\cdot(T_\text{surf}-T_\infty) \quad \text{(crossed out — this overestimates } \dot{Q}\text{)}$$

Because $\dot{Q}$ can't simply scale with $N$ and $T_\text{surf}-T_\infty$: the fluid warms up as it passes through the bank.

Instead, use the **log-mean temperature difference**:

$$\boxed{\Delta T_{lm} = \frac{(T_s - T_i) - (T_s - T_o)}{\ln\!\left(\frac{T_s - T_i}{T_s - T_o}\right)}} \quad = \text{"log-mean temperature difference"}$$

a better representation of the average local $\Delta T$, where $T_i$ = inlet temperature, $T_o$ = outlet temperature of external flow.

Find $T_o$ from:

$$\frac{T_s - T_o}{T_s - T_i} \approx \exp\!\left(\frac{-\pi D N \overline{h}}{\rho V N_T S_T c_p}\right)$$

(Here $N_T$ = number of tubes per row, $V$ = free-stream velocity.)

Then:

$$\boxed{\dot{Q} = N\overline{h}\,\pi DL\cdot\Delta T_{lm}} \qquad \text{(Bank of }N\text{ tubes)}$$
