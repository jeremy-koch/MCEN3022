5  Internal Convection

Now we turn to “internal convection” – fluids flowing inside channels, ducts, and pipes. This is the geometry that shows up in coolant flowing through a heat exchanger, water running through a solar collector, and air being pushed through a ventilation duct.

We just finished external convection, where we typically had an obvious \(\Delta T=T_s-T_\infty\). Internal convection is going to complicate that picture a bit – once a fluid is confined inside a pipe, the temperature of the fluid itself is changing along the way, so there is no single \(T_\infty\) to use in our convective heat transfer equation.

The thermodynamics you already know will re-enter the picture here: \(\dot{Q} = \dot{m}c_p\Delta T\) describes how much the bulk temperature rises. But thermodynamics glosses over a lot of physics, and we’ll spend the first part of this chapter being more careful. After that, the approach mirrors what we did for external convection: look up a Nusselt Number correlation, extract \(h\), and compute the heat transfer. The new wrinkle is that \(h\) will let us track how the mean fluid temperature evolves along the pipe — not just at the outlet.

Note

Correlations in this chapter are again drawn from Incropera, DeWitt, Bergman, and Lavine “Fundamentals of Heat and Mass Transfer”, sixth edition (2007).

5.1 Think First

  2. From fluid mechanics: for flow inside a pipe, what does the velocity profile look like? What is the Reynolds Number for pipe flow, and what is the critical value for the transition to turbulence?
  3. From the last chapter: how did we define \(h\)? What factors did it depend on, and how did we write \(\dot{Q}\) in terms of \(h\)?

Look Ahead

  1. A fluid entering a heated pipe starts cold and heats up as it travels. Near the inlet, how do you expect the local heat transfer coefficient \(h\) to compare to its value far downstream? Will \(h\) vary with position at all? Increase/decrease indefinitely? Asymptote?
  2. There are two common boundary conditions for a heated pipe: constant wall temperature \(T_s\), and constant heat flux \(q''_s\). In one of these cases, the mean temperature of the flow will increase linearly, in the other it will increase but as an exponential decay. Which is which?
  3. Think about a very viscous fluid like oil (\(\text{Pr} \approx 100\)) versus air (\(\text{Pr} \approx 0.7\)) flowing through the same heated pipe at the same Reynolds Number. Which fluid do you expect to take longer (more pipe length) to reach a “fully developed” state, and why?

5.2 Thermodynamics

This whole chapter is about flow through pipes. There will be some mass flow rate \(\dot{m}\), and we’ll say that the flow is proceeding in the axial direction given by coordinate \(x\).

A typical problem in thermodynamics is: we have a known \(\dot{Q}\) added to this flow over some distance \(\Delta x\), how much does the temperature change? The answer is given by

\[ \dot{Q} = \dot{m} c_p \Delta T. \tag{5.1}\]

But what have we ignored here?

  1. How can we just say how much heat flows into a system? We can burn a fuel+air mixture with known heat content, but there will be some thermal resistance/inefficiencies that means not all of that heat makes it into the working medium. And it’s a convection process… doesn’t \(h\) depend on speed? Can we really adjust \(\dot{Q}\sim h\) and \(\dot{m}\) independently?
  2. Is it true that the entire cross-section of the flow changes temperature at the same time? We talked about the thermal boundary layer last chapter, which is probably relevant. But if there is indeed a temperature distribution for a given cross-section, what are the values of \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) in \(\Delta T=T_2-T_1\)?

To understand the nuances of the process, we need to be much more careful than we were in thermodynamics!

5.2.1 Mean flow

Let’s start with the temperature distribution question: we are not going to simply assume uniform flow, we are going to allow for variation in speed and temperature within a cross-section located at axial position \(x\). (We will continue to assume constant properties, though.) Define the mean velocity \(u_m\) as

\[ u_m(x) = \frac{1}{\rho A_c}\int \rho\, u(r,x)\,dA_c. \]

Then, if our goal is to use a framework like that in Equation 5.1, we need to come up with a temperature that gives the average enthalpy at \(x\). That is: we want a mean temperature \(T_m\) that describes the average enthalpy, and we get it via:

In thermodynamics we use \(h\) for enthalpy… in heat transfer we use \(h\) for convection coefficient. So we jump immediately to the model that the change in enthalpy is proportional to the change in temperature.

\[ \dot{m} c_p T_m = \int \rho\, u\, c_p\, T\,dA_c \]

where \(u\) and \(T\) are functions of position within the cross-section, e.g. for a circular pipe: \(u(r,\theta)\).

TipFor a cylindrical pipe…

\[ T_m(x) = \frac{2}{u_m R^2}\int_0^R u(r;x)\,T(r;x)\,r\,dr. \]

5.3 Heat transfer in pipes

Recall the goal of \(h\): it groups together the boundary layer and fluid properties such that we can write the total heat transfer as a product of \(h\), the surface area, and a temperature difference between two known temperatures (e.g. \(T_\text{s}-T_\infty\)). In pipe flow, there is no \(T_\infty\)… instead we try to use \(T_m\) and write

\[ q'' = h(T_s - T_m) \]

and, since \(T_m\) is definitely going to depend on \(x\), \(q''\) will depend on \(x\). So… it is a fairly simple description for the heat flux, assuming we can make it work. Hopefully \(h\) isn’t going to depend on \(x\) or anything like that – it will turn into a complicated analysis if so, mandating an integral instead of a simple \(hA\Delta T\).

5.3.1 Fully developed flow

What is a practical definition for “steady” and “transient” when heat is being added to a flow? A given parcel of fluid is going to be increasing in temperature as it moves through a heated section of pipe.

We will say that a flow is “fully developed” when the relative shape of the temperature profile is unchanging with axial position: if every radial location is increasing in temperature at 2 K/m, the mean temperature is also changing at 2 K/m. Mathematically:

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{T_s(x) - T(r,x)}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right]_\text{fd} = 0. \]

The fact that the partial derivative with respect to \(x\) is equal to zero tells us the fraction from above does not depend on \(x\), but it will depend on \(r\). For mathematical clarity, let’s give a symbol to the fraction above (when it is fully developed):

\[ M(r) \equiv \left[\frac{T_s(x) - T(r,x)}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right]_\text{fd}. \]

Then we can write

\[ \frac{\partial M}{\partial r}=\left(\frac{1}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right)\left(-\frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right). \]

OK, why should we care about this mathematical observation? Watch this: bring in Fourier’s Law at the pipe wall, and set it equal to our desired form \(h(T_s-T_m)\):

\[ q_s'' = k_f\frac{\partial T}{\partial r} = h(T_s(x) - T_m(x)). \]

From above we can substitute-in

\[ q_s'' = -k_f M'(r)\cdot(T_s(x) - T_m(x)) = h(T_s(x) - T_m(x)) \]

and can cancel the \(\Delta T\) to conclude \[ h = -k_f M'(r)\quad h \text{ is not a function of }x. \]

Really interesting! It turns out that, even though \(T_s\) and \(T_m\) are functions of \(x\), \(h\) is not! Once we know the flow speed, geometry, and fluid, \(h\) will be constant for fully developed flow.

Correlations are summarized below.

5.3.2 Entrance Region

However, we will not be limiting ourselves to cases where the flow is fully developed. A fluid entering a heated section of pipe will experience an “entrance region” where the flow is developing. The axial distance to being fully developed (in a velocity sense) is given by

\[ \frac{L_{fd}}{D} \approx \begin{cases}0.05\,\text{Re}_D & \text{for a flow that will be laminar} \\ 10 & \text{for turbulent}\end{cases} \]

where \(\text{Re}_D = \rho u_m D/\mu\) is the Reynolds Number and \(\text{Re}_{D,\text{crit}} \approx 2300\).

For the thermal entrance length:

\[ \frac{x_{fd}}{D} \approx \begin{cases}0.05\,\text{Re}_D\cdot\text{Pr} & \text{laminar} \\ 10 & \text{turbulent}\end{cases} \]

Recall with the flat plate geometry that the boundary layer thickness scales with \(1/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}\) and the thermal boundary layer thickness scales with \(1/\sqrt{\text{Pr}\text{Re}_x}\). The form of the thermal entrance length is not surprising!

5.4 Pipe flow correlations for different shapes and turbulence

We again collect \(h\) values in Nusselt Number correlations. We learned that \(h\) does not depend on position, but it does depend on the Reynolds Number and whether the flow is fully developed.

Hydrodynamic:

\[ \frac{L_\text{fd}}{D}= \begin{cases} 0.05\,\text{Re}_D & \text{if fully developed flow will be laminar, } \text{Re}_D<2300 \\ 10 & \text{if fully developed flow will be turbulent} \end{cases} \]

Thermal:

\[ \frac{L_\text{fd}}{D}= \begin{cases} 0.05\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr} & \text{if fully developed flow will be laminar, } \text{Re}_D<2300 \\ 10 & \text{if fully developed flow will be turbulent} \end{cases} \]

    \[\overline{\text{Nu}}_D=3.66+\frac{0.0668(D/L)\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr}}{1+0.04\left[(D/L)\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr}\right]^{2/3}}\]

We might notice that the 3.66 factor in the first correlation is precisely the Nusselt Number for fully developed flow with \(T_\text{S}=\text{constant}\). A reasonable approximation, then, might be to replace this factor with 4.36, the Nusselt Number for fully developed flow with \(q''_\text{S}=\text{constant}\).

5.4.3.2 Turbulent flow

Turbulent, \(\text{Re}_D>2300\):

  • For small temperature differences:

    \[\text{Nu}_D=0.023\,\text{Re}^{4/5}_D\,\text{Pr}^n\]

    where \(n=0.4\) for heating (\(T_\text{s}>T_\text{m}\)) and \(n=0.3\) for cooling. Applicable for both uniform surface temperature and uniform heat flux.

  • For large temperature differences:

    \[\text{Nu}_D=0.027\,\text{Re}^{4/5}_D\,\text{Pr}^{1/3}\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_\text{s}}\right)^{0.14}\]

    where \(\mu_\text{s}\) is evaluated at the surface temperature. Applicable for both uniform surface temperature and uniform heat flux.

  • Improved correlation, but more complicated to use:

    \[\text{Nu}_D=\frac{(f/8)(\text{Re}_D-1000)\,\text{Pr}}{1+12.7(f/8)^{1/2}(\text{Pr}^{2/3}-1)}\]

    where \(f\) is the friction factor from the Moody diagram.

5.5 Temperature Variation Along the Pipe

How much pipe needs to be heated in order to reach a given operating temperature? Now that we have \(h\), we can answer this question.

TipVideo

The content here is pretty similar to this linked video.

5.5.1 Known surface temperature \(T_s\)

5.5.2 1. \(T_s = \text{constant}\)

Write an energy balance relating thermodynamics and heat transfer

\[ d\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,dT_m = h\cdot P\cdot dx\cdot(T_s - T_m) \]

where \(P\) is the perimeter. Rearranging and integrating

\[ \int\frac{dT_m}{T_s - T_m} = \int\frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,dx \]

\[ -\ln(T_s - T_m)\bigg|_{T_{m,i}}^{T_m(x)} = \frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x \]

\[ \ln\left[\frac{T_s - T_m(x)}{T_s - T_{m,i}}\right] = -\frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x \]

we are left with

\[ \frac{T_s - T_m(x)}{T_s - T_{m,i}} = \exp\!\left(\frac{-hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x\right). \]

5.5.3 Known heat flux \(q''_s\)

Again, write an energy balance:

\[ \dot{m}\,c_p\,dT_m = q_s''\cdot P\cdot dx. \]

Rearrange and integrate

\[ \int dT_m = \int\frac{q_s''\cdot P}{\dot{m}\,c_p}\,dx \]

leaving

\[ T_m(x) = \frac{q_s''\,P}{\dot{m}\,c_p}\,x + T_{m,i}. \]

Notice that this does not require \(h\)!

## Thermodynamics

This whole chapter is about flow through pipes. There will be some mass flow rate $\dot{m}$, and we'll say that the flow is proceeding in the axial direction given by coordinate $x$.

A typical problem in thermodynamics is: we have a known $\dot{Q}$ added to this flow over some distance $\Delta x$, how much does the temperature change? The answer is given by

$$
\dot{Q} = \dot{m} c_p \Delta T.
$$ {#eq-thermo}

But what have we ignored here?

1. How can we just say how much heat flows into a system? We can burn a fuel+air mixture with known heat content, but there will be some thermal resistance/inefficiencies that means not all of that heat makes it into the working medium. And it's a convection process... doesn't $h$ depend on speed? Can we really adjust $\dot{Q}\sim h$ and $\dot{m}$ independently?
2. Is it true that the entire cross-section of the flow changes temperature at the same time? We talked about the thermal boundary layer last chapter, which is probably relevant. But if there is indeed a temperature distribution for a given cross-section, what are the values of $T_1$ and $T_2$ in $\Delta T=T_2-T_1$?

To understand the nuances of the process, we need to be much more careful than we were in thermodynamics!

### Mean flow

Let's start with the temperature distribution question: we are not going to simply assume uniform flow, we are going to allow for variation in speed and temperature within a cross-section located at axial position $x$. (We will continue to assume constant properties, though.) Define the mean velocity $u_m$ as

$$
u_m(x) = \frac{1}{\rho A_c}\int \rho\, u(r,x)\,dA_c.
$$

Then, if our goal is to use a framework like that in @eq-thermo, we need to come up with a temperature that gives the average enthalpy at $x$. That is: we want a mean temperature $T_m$ that describes the average enthalpy, and we get it via:

::: {.column-margin}
In thermodynamics we use $h$ for enthalpy... in heat transfer we use $h$ for convection coefficient. So we jump immediately to the model that the change in enthalpy is proportional to the change in temperature.
:::

$$
\dot{m} c_p T_m = \int \rho\, u\, c_p\, T\,dA_c
$$

where $u$ and $T$ are functions of position within the cross-section, e.g. for a circular pipe: $u(r,\theta)$.

::: {.callout-tip}
### For a cylindrical pipe...

$$
T_m(x) = \frac{2}{u_m R^2}\int_0^R u(r;x)\,T(r;x)\,r\,dr.
$$ 
:::



## Heat transfer in pipes

Recall the goal of $h$: it groups together the boundary layer and fluid properties such that we can write the total heat transfer as a product of $h$, the surface area, and a temperature difference between two known temperatures (e.g. $T_\text{s}-T_\infty$). In pipe flow, there is no $T_\infty$... instead we try to use $T_m$ and write

$$
q'' = h(T_s - T_m)
$$

and, since $T_m$ is definitely going to depend on $x$, $q''$ will depend on $x$. So... it is a fairly simple description for the heat flux, assuming we can make it work. Hopefully $h$ isn't going to depend on $x$ or anything like that -- it will turn into a complicated analysis if so, mandating an integral instead of a simple $hA\Delta T$.

### Fully developed flow

What is a practical definition for "steady" and "transient" when heat is being added to a flow? A given parcel of fluid is going to be increasing in temperature as it moves through a heated section of pipe.

We will say that a flow is "fully developed" when the relative shape of the temperature profile is unchanging with axial position: if every radial location is increasing in temperature at 2 K/m, the mean temperature is also changing at 2 K/m. Mathematically:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{T_s(x) - T(r,x)}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right]_\text{fd} = 0.
$$

The fact that the partial derivative with respect to $x$ is equal to zero tells us the fraction from above does not depend on $x$, but it will depend on $r$. 
For mathematical clarity, let's give a symbol to the fraction above (when it is fully developed):

$$
M(r) \equiv \left[\frac{T_s(x) - T(r,x)}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right]_\text{fd}.
$$

Then we can write

$$
\frac{\partial M}{\partial r}=\left(\frac{1}{T_s(x) - T_m(x)}\right)\left(-\frac{\partial T}{\partial r}\right).
$$

OK, why should we care about this mathematical observation? Watch this: bring in Fourier's Law at the pipe wall, and set it equal to our desired form $h(T_s-T_m)$:

$$
q_s'' = k_f\frac{\partial T}{\partial r} = h(T_s(x) - T_m(x)).
$$

From above we can substitute-in 

$$
q_s'' = -k_f M'(r)\cdot(T_s(x) - T_m(x)) = h(T_s(x) - T_m(x))
$$

and can cancel the $\Delta T$ to conclude
$$
h = -k_f M'(r)\quad h \text{ is not a function of }x.
$$

Really interesting! It turns out that, even though $T_s$ and $T_m$ are functions of $x$, $h$ is not! Once we know the flow speed, geometry, and fluid, $h$ will be constant for fully developed flow.

$$

::: {.column-margin}
Recall with the flat plate geometry that the boundary layer thickness scales with $1/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$ and the thermal boundary layer thickness scales with $1/\sqrt{\text{Pr}\text{Re}_x}$. The form of the thermal entrance length is not surprising!
:::


## Pipe flow correlations for different shapes and turbulence

We again collect $h$ values in Nusselt Number correlations. We learned that $h$ does not depend on position, but it does depend on the Reynolds Number and whether the flow is fully developed.

### Entrance Lengths

Hydrodynamic:

$$
\frac{L_\text{fd}}{D}=
\begin{cases}
  0.05\,\text{Re}_D & \text{if fully developed flow will be laminar, } \text{Re}_D<2300 \\
  10 & \text{if fully developed flow will be turbulent}
\end{cases}
$$

Thermal:

$$
\frac{L_\text{fd}}{D}=
\begin{cases}
  0.05\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr} & \text{if fully developed flow will be laminar, } \text{Re}_D<2300 \\
  10 & \text{if fully developed flow will be turbulent}
\end{cases}
$$

### Entrance Region Correlations

Laminar:

- If the flow is hydrodynamically fully developed, but not thermally:

  $$\overline{\text{Nu}}_D=3.66+\frac{0.0668(D/L)\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr}}{1+0.04\left[(D/L)\,\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr}\right]^{2/3}}$$

  This correlation is also typically used for fluids where $\text{Pr}\geq 5$, as the viscous diffusion in such a case is at least $5\times$ more rapid than the thermal diffusion, and so, hydrodynamically, it develops quickly.

- For a "combined entry length" that is developing both hydrodynamically and thermally:

  $$\overline{\text{Nu}}_D=1.86\left(\frac{\text{Re}_D\,\text{Pr}}{L/D}\right)^{1/3}\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_\text{S}}\right)^{0.14}$$

  This is used for $\text{Pr}<5$.

Technically both of these correlations are for $T_\text{S}=\text{constant}$. The book does not provide a correlation for the case where $q''_\text{S}=\text{constant}$, instead providing a plot for the local Nusselt Number. We might notice that the 3.66 factor in the first correlation is precisely the Nusselt Number for fully developed flow with $T_\text{S}=\text{constant}$. A reasonable approximation, then, might be to replace this factor with 4.36, the Nusselt Number for fully developed flow with $q''_\text{S}=\text{constant}$.

Turbulent: Typically we assume it is the same as for fully developed flow (given below).

### Fully Developed Correlations

#### Laminar

$\text{Re}_D<2300$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Nu}_D\equiv \frac{hD}{k}&=3.66\qquad \text{for } T_\text{s}=\text{constant}\\
&=4.36\qquad \text{for } q''_\text{s}=\text{constant}
\end{aligned}
$$

##### The effect of cross section

For flows with non-circular cross sections, we use the "hydraulic diameter" in the Nusselt Number:

$$D_\text{H}\equiv \frac{4A_c}{P}$$

We also represent the friction factor in an analogous way: for circular laminar pipe flow it is $f=64/\text{Re}_D$, and we update the coefficient "64" according to the hydraulic diameter:

| Cross Section | $b/a$ | $\text{Nu}_{D_\text{h}}$ (uniform $T_\text{S}$) | $\text{Nu}_{D_\text{h}}$ (uniform $q''_\text{s}$) | $f\,\text{Re}_{D_\text{h}}$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| circle | -- | 3.66 | 4.36 | 64 |
| square | 1.0 | 2.98 | 3.61 | 57 |
| rectangle | 1.43 | 3.08 | 3.73 | 59 |
| rectangle | 2.0 | 3.39 | 4.12 | 62 |
| rectangle | 3.0 | 3.96 | 4.79 | 69 |
| rectangle | 4.0 | 4.44 | 5.33 | 73 |
| rectangle | 8.0 | 5.60 | 6.49 | 82 |
| rectangle | $\infty$ | 7.54 | 8.23 | 96 |
| rectangle, insulated below | $\infty$ | 4.86 | 5.39 | 96 |
| triangle | -- | 2.49 | 3.11 | 53 |

##### Annulus

In the following, the diameter used in the Nu correlation is $D_\text{H}=D_\text{o}-D_\text{i}$.

For flow through an annulus, with one surface insulated and the other at constant temperature:

| $D_i/D_o$ | $\text{Nu}_i$ | $\text{Nu}_o$ |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | -- | 3.66 |
| 0.05 | 17.46 | 4.06 |
| 0.10 | 11.56 | 4.11 |
| 0.25 | 7.37 | 4.23 |
| 0.50 | 5.74 | 4.43 |
| 0.999 | 4.86 | 4.86 |

For uniform heat flux at both surfaces, a more complicated correlation is developed with

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Nu}_i&=\frac{\text{Nu}_{ii}}{1-(q''_o/q''_i)\,\theta^*_i}\\
\text{Nu}_o&=\frac{\text{Nu}_{oo}}{1-(q''_i/q''_o)\,\theta^*_o}
\end{aligned}
$$

| $D_i/D_o$ | $\text{Nu}_{ii}$ | $\text{Nu}_{oo}$ | $\theta^*_i$ | $\theta^*_o$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | -- | 4.364 | $\infty$ | 0 |
| 0.05 | 17.81 | 4.792 | 2.18 | 0.0294 |
| 0.10 | 11.91 | 4.834 | 1.383 | 0.0562 |
| 0.20 | 8.499 | 4.833 | 0.905 | 0.1041 |
| 0.40 | 6.583 | 4.979 | 0.603 | 0.1823 |
| 0.60 | 5.912 | 5.099 | 0.473 | 0.2455 |
| 0.80 | 5.58 | 5.24 | 0.401 | 0.299 |
| 0.999 | 5.385 | 5.385 | 0.346 | 0.346 |

Note: $q''_i$ and $q''_o$ may be positive or negative. In the above, we call both positive when they are flowing into the annulus.

Example of using this chart: Suppose the outside of the annulus is insulated. Then $q''_o=0$. For a concentric tube heat exchanger, especially in the counterflow configuration, it is reasonable to claim $q''_i=\text{constant} = \dot{Q}/A$ (we might determine this from thermodynamics... the heat needed to change a fluid's temperature by a known amount). Then, from the geometry $D_i/D_o$, we can read off all necessary values to determine $\text{Nu}_i$, and from there, $h_i$.

#### Turbulent flow

Turbulent, $\text{Re}_D>2300$:

- For small temperature differences:

  $$\text{Nu}_D=0.023\,\text{Re}^{4/5}_D\,\text{Pr}^n$$

  where $n=0.4$ for heating ($T_\text{s}>T_\text{m}$) and $n=0.3$ for cooling. Applicable for both uniform surface temperature and uniform heat flux.

- For large temperature differences:

  $$\text{Nu}_D=0.027\,\text{Re}^{4/5}_D\,\text{Pr}^{1/3}\left(\frac{\mu}{\mu_\text{s}}\right)^{0.14}$$

  where $\mu_\text{s}$ is evaluated at the surface temperature. Applicable for both uniform surface temperature and uniform heat flux.

- Improved correlation, but more complicated to use:

  $$\text{Nu}_D=\frac{(f/8)(\text{Re}_D-1000)\,\text{Pr}}{1+12.7(f/8)^{1/2}(\text{Pr}^{2/3}-1)}$$

  where $f$ is the friction factor from the Moody diagram.






## Temperature Variation Along the Pipe


How much pipe needs to be heated in order to reach a given operating temperature? Now that we have $h$, we can answer this question.

::: {.callout-tip}
### Video

The content here is pretty similar to [this linked video](https://canvas.colorado.edu/courses/136150/pages/pipe-flow-temperature-variation).
:::

### Known surface temperature $T_s$

### 1. $T_s = \text{constant}$

Write an energy balance relating thermodynamics and heat transfer

$$
d\dot{Q} = \dot{m}\,c_p\,dT_m = h\cdot P\cdot dx\cdot(T_s - T_m)
$$

where $P$ is the perimeter. Rearranging and integrating

$$
\int\frac{dT_m}{T_s - T_m} = \int\frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,dx
$$

$$
-\ln(T_s - T_m)\bigg|_{T_{m,i}}^{T_m(x)} = \frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x
$$

$$
\ln\left[\frac{T_s - T_m(x)}{T_s - T_{m,i}}\right] = -\frac{hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x
$$

we are left with

$$
\frac{T_s - T_m(x)}{T_s - T_{m,i}} = \exp\!\left(\frac{-hP}{\dot{m}c_p}\,x\right).
$$

### Known heat flux $q''_s$

Again, write an energy balance:

$$
\dot{m}\,c_p\,dT_m = q_s''\cdot P\cdot dx.
$$

Rearrange and integrate

$$
\int dT_m = \int\frac{q_s''\cdot P}{\dot{m}\,c_p}\,dx
$$

leaving

$$
T_m(x) = \frac{q_s''\,P}{\dot{m}\,c_p}\,x + T_{m,i}.
$$

Notice that this does not require $h$!
